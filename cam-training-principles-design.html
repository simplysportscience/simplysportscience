Training Principles & Design

The principles that turn training from just working hard into a structured programme that actually produces adaptation.

Key Terms

OverloadTraining must place a greater demand on the body than it's used to.
SpecificityTraining should match the demands of the target sport.
ProgressionOverload should increase gradually as fitness improves.
ReversibilityFitness gains are lost if training stops or reduces significantly.
FITTFrequency, Intensity, Time, Type — the four training variables.

Principles of Training

  • Overload — training must be harder than what the body is accustomed to, to force adaptation.
  • Specificity — training should match the movement patterns and energy systems of the target sport.
  • Progression — overload should increase gradually as fitness improves.
  • Reversibility — fitness gains are lost if training stops or reduces significantly.
  • Individualisation — training should be tailored to the individual's needs and starting point.

The FITT Framework

Frequency — how often training occurs; Intensity — how hard, e.g. %HRmax or %1RM; Time — the duration of each session; Type — the mode of training used, matched to specificity.

Periodization

Training is organised into nested cycles: a macrocycle covers a full season/year; a mesocycle is a block of several weeks with a specific focus; a microcycle is typically a single week — allowing fitness to be built systematically while managing fatigue and peaking for key competitions.

Exam tip: "Discuss training design for [a named sport]" wants the principles — specificity especially — applied to that sport's actual energy system and movement demands, not just listed generically.

Practice Check
1. Define the principle of progression. +
The principle that overload should be increased gradually over time, in line with improving fitness, to continue producing adaptation.
2. Describe the FITT framework. +
Model answer points:
  • Frequency — how often training sessions occur.
  • Intensity — how hard each session is, e.g. as a percentage of maximum heart rate or 1 rep max.
  • Time — the duration of each session.
  • Type — the specific mode or method of training used.
3. Explain the principle of reversibility. +
Model answer points:
  • Fitness gains made through training are gradually lost if training stops, or is significantly reduced.
  • This is why maintaining a minimum level of training is important during periods such as injury recovery or the off-season.
4. Discuss how the principle of specificity should influence the training design of a 400m sprinter. +
Model answer points:
  • Training should predominantly target the ATP-PC and anaerobic glycolytic energy systems, since these dominate a 400m effort.
  • Movement patterns, muscle groups and speed of contraction used in training should closely mirror sprinting technique.
  • General aerobic conditioning has limited value, since it does not reflect the specific demands of the event.
