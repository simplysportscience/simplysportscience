A.2 Hydration and Nutrition

What actually goes into fuelling the body for exercise — from fluid balance to the three energy systems that keep muscles supplied with ATP.

Key Terms

DehydrationA state of fluid deficit, where fluid losses exceed fluid intake.
MacronutrientCarbohydrate, fat or protein — nutrients providing energy, needed in large amounts.
ATPAdenosine triphosphate — the only usable energy source for muscle contraction.
ATP-PC SystemAnaerobic system using stored phosphocreatine; fast but lasts ~10 seconds.
Aerobic SystemUses oxygen to break down fuel via the Krebs cycle; slow but high-yield.

A.2.1 Water and Electrolyte Balance

Water enables thermoregulation, transports nutrients and waste, and maintains blood volume. As little as 2% body mass loss through fluid can impair performance — reducing plasma volume and therefore stroke volume, forcing heart rate to compensate (cardiovascular drift).

Hydration strategy: ensure euhydration before exercise, replace fluid at a rate close to sweat loss during exercise, and rehydrate with fluid and electrolytes afterward. Overhydration with plain water risks hyponatraemia — dangerously diluted blood sodium.

A.2.2 Fuelling for Health and Performance

Carbohydrate is the primary fuel for moderate-to-high intensity exercise, stored as glycogen. Fat is the most energy-dense macronutrient, dominant at rest and low intensity. Protein is primarily for tissue repair, not fuel. Athletes often use carbohydrate loading before endurance events and combined carbohydrate/protein intake afterward to replenish glycogen and support repair.

A.2.3 Energy Systems

Muscles can only use ATP directly, and store barely 1–2 seconds' worth — everything after that depends on resynthesis via three systems:

SystemOxygen?DurationATP Yield
ATP-PCAnaerobic~10 secondsVery low, very fast
Anaerobic GlycolysisAnaerobic~10-60 secondsLow (2 ATP), fast
AerobicAerobic60 seconds+High (36-38 ATP), slow

All three systems contribute simultaneously — it's the balance between them that shifts with intensity and duration, from an ATP-PC-dominant sprint to an aerobic-dominant marathon.

Exam tip: "Discuss the relative contribution of energy systems" wants the balance across a specific named event, not just a description of each system in isolation.

Practice Check
1. Explain the risk of hyponatraemia during prolonged exercise. +
Model answer points:
  • Drinking large volumes of plain water without replacing electrolytes can dilute blood sodium concentration.
  • This is a genuine risk in ultra-endurance events and can be dangerous, causing confusion or seizures in severe cases.
2. Describe the role of carbohydrate as a fuel source, and explain a strategy athletes use to maximise its availability. +
Model answer points:
  • Carbohydrate is stored as glycogen and is the primary fuel for moderate-to-high intensity exercise.
  • Carbohydrate loading in the days before an endurance event maximises glycogen stores ahead of competition.
3. Explain the resynthesis of ATP by the ATP-PC system. +
Model answer points:
  • Creatine kinase breaks down stored phosphocreatine into phosphate and creatine, releasing energy.
  • This energy is used to rebuild ATP from ADP and phosphate.
  • The system is anaerobic and very fast, but limited to around 10 seconds of maximal effort.
4. Discuss the relative contribution of the three energy systems during a 400m sprint compared to a marathon. +
Model answer points:
  • A 400m sprint draws heavily on the ATP-PC system initially, then shifts to anaerobic glycolysis as the dominant system.
  • The aerobic system makes only a small contribution during a 400m race, given the limited time available.
  • A marathon relies almost entirely on the aerobic system throughout, since it can sustain ATP production for hours.
  • ATP-PC and glycolysis play only a minor role in a marathon, mainly at the start or a finishing sprint.
