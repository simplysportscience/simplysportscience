The Cardiovascular System

How the heart, blood and blood vessels work together to deliver oxygen to working muscles — and how the whole system changes, both in the moment and over months of training.

Key Terms

Cardiac Output (Q)The volume of blood pumped by the heart in one minute. Q = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume.
Stroke Volume (SV)The volume of blood ejected from the left ventricle in a single heartbeat.
SA NodeThe heart's natural pacemaker; sets the resting rate of contraction via intrinsic control.
Vascular ShuntingThe redistribution of blood flow away from non-essential organs (e.g. the gut) and towards working skeletal muscle during exercise.
Cardiovascular DriftA gradual rise in heart rate and fall in stroke volume during prolonged, sub-maximal exercise, despite a constant workload.
BradycardiaA resting heart rate below 60 bpm — a common adaptation seen in endurance-trained athletes.
VO₂ MaxThe maximum volume of oxygen the body can consume and use per minute, relative to body mass (ml/kg/min).

Blood Composition

Blood is made up of plasma (the liquid portion, roughly 55% of blood volume) and three types of cells:

  • Erythrocytes (red blood cells) — contain haemoglobin, which binds to oxygen and transports it around the body. Erythrocytes make up roughly 40–45% of blood volume, a proportion known as haematocrit.
  • Leucocytes (white blood cells) — part of the immune system; they identify and destroy pathogens.
  • Platelets — responsible for clotting, preventing excessive blood loss after injury.

Plasma itself also transports nutrients, hormones, electrolytes and waste products (like carbon dioxide) around the body.

Heart Structure & the Path of Blood

The heart has four chambers (right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, left ventricle) and four valves (tricuspid, pulmonary, bicuspid/mitral, aortic) that keep blood flowing in one direction.

Blood follows two connected circuits:

  • Pulmonary circulation — deoxygenated blood leaves the right ventricle via the pulmonary artery to the lungs, picks up oxygen, and returns to the left atrium via the pulmonary vein.
  • Systemic circulation — oxygenated blood leaves the left ventricle via the aorta to the body, delivers oxygen to tissues, and returns to the right atrium via the vena cava.

Exam tip: If a question asks you to "describe" the path of blood through the heart, examiners want the correct sequence of chambers, valves and vessels — not just a list of parts. Practise saying it as one continuous journey.

Regulation of Heart Rate

Heart rate is controlled in two ways:

  • Intrinsic control — the SA node initiates each heartbeat automatically, without input from the nervous system. The signal spreads through the atria, passes through the AV node, then down the Bundle of His and Purkinje fibres to trigger ventricular contraction.
  • Extrinsic control — the cardiovascular control centre (in the medulla) adjusts heart rate via the autonomic nervous system. The sympathetic nervous system increases heart rate (e.g. during exercise); the parasympathetic nervous system (via the vagus nerve) decreases it. Hormonal input — mainly adrenaline — also increases heart rate, reinforcing the sympathetic response.

Cardiac Output & the Exercise Response

Cardiac output (Q) = Heart Rate (HR) × Stroke Volume (SV). At rest, Q is typically around 5 litres/minute. During maximal exercise, Q can rise to 20–40 litres/minute, driven by increases in both HR and SV.

Stroke volume increases with exercise intensity up to a point (typically 40–60% of maximum effort), after which it plateaus — meaning further increases in cardiac output come almost entirely from a rising heart rate.

Blood Pressure

Blood pressure is recorded as two values: systolic (pressure when the ventricles contract) over diastolic (pressure when the heart relaxes between beats), e.g. 120/80 mmHg at rest.

  • Dynamic (isotonic) exercise — systolic pressure rises in proportion to intensity; diastolic pressure stays roughly the same or falls slightly, as blood vessels in working muscle dilate.
  • Static (isometric) exercise — both systolic and diastolic pressure rise sharply, because sustained muscle contraction compresses blood vessels and increases resistance to flow.

Redistribution of Blood During Exercise (Vascular Shunting)

At rest, blood flow is spread fairly evenly, with a large share going to the digestive system and kidneys. During exercise, blood is redirected — vasoconstriction narrows vessels supplying non-essential organs (gut, kidneys), while vasodilation widens vessels supplying skeletal muscle, the heart and the skin (for temperature regulation). This is known as vascular shunting, and it dramatically increases the proportion of cardiac output reaching working muscle — from around 15–20% at rest to over 80% during intense exercise.

Cardiovascular Adaptations to Endurance Training

With sustained endurance training, the cardiovascular system adapts in several measurable ways:

  • Cardiac hypertrophy — the left ventricle wall thickens and its chamber enlarges, allowing it to fill with and eject more blood per beat.
  • Increased stroke volume — both at rest and during exercise, as a direct result of the larger, stronger ventricle.
  • Bradycardia — resting heart rate falls, because the same cardiac output can be achieved with fewer, more powerful beats.
  • Increased capillarisation — more capillaries form around trained muscle fibres, improving oxygen delivery and extraction.
  • Faster recovery — heart rate returns to resting levels more quickly after exercise ends.

Exam tip: "Discuss" questions on this topic want more than a list — pair each adaptation with its effect. For example: "increased stroke volume, which allows cardiac output to be maintained at a lower heart rate, reducing myocardial oxygen demand."

VO₂ Max

VO₂ max is the maximum volume of oxygen the body can take in, transport and use per minute, expressed relative to body mass (ml/kg/min). It represents the functional capacity of the whole oxygen transport chain — lungs, heart, blood and muscles working together.

VO₂ max varies with:

  • Training status — trained individuals have a higher VO₂ max than untrained individuals.
  • Sex — males typically have higher VO₂ max values than females, partly due to differences in haemoglobin levels and body composition.
  • Age — VO₂ max tends to decline with age after early adulthood.
  • Mode of exercise — VO₂ max measured while running is usually higher than while cycling or arm-cranking, since running uses a larger total muscle mass.

Practice Check
1. Define cardiac output. +
The volume of blood pumped by the heart in one minute, calculated as heart rate multiplied by stroke volume (Q = HR × SV).
2. Describe the intrinsic regulation of heart rate. +
Model answer points:
  • The SA node acts as the heart's natural pacemaker, generating an electrical impulse without external input.
  • The impulse spreads across the atria, causing them to contract.
  • The impulse reaches the AV node, which briefly delays it before passing it to the Bundle of His.
  • The impulse travels down the Purkinje fibres, causing the ventricles to contract.
3. Explain how blood flow is redistributed during exercise. +
Model answer points:
  • Vasoconstriction narrows blood vessels supplying non-essential organs, such as the gut and kidneys.
  • Vasodilation widens blood vessels supplying skeletal muscle, the heart, and skin.
  • This shift — vascular shunting — increases the proportion of cardiac output reaching working muscles from around 15–20% at rest to over 80% during intense exercise.
4. Compare the distribution of blood at rest and during exercise. +
Model answer points:
  • At rest, blood flow is spread more evenly, with a large proportion directed to the digestive system and kidneys.
  • During exercise, a much greater proportion is redirected to skeletal muscle to meet the increased demand for oxygen.
  • Blood flow to the skin also increases during exercise, to support thermoregulation.
  • Flow to the gut and kidneys decreases in both cases, as it's diverted elsewhere.
5. Discuss the cardiovascular adaptations that occur as a result of endurance training. +
Model answer points:
  • Cardiac hypertrophy — the left ventricle enlarges and thickens, increasing its pumping capacity.
  • Increased stroke volume at rest and during exercise, meaning more blood is ejected per beat.
  • Bradycardia — resting heart rate decreases, as the same cardiac output can be achieved with fewer beats.
  • Increased capillarisation around trained muscle fibres, improving oxygen delivery and extraction.
  • Together, these adaptations reduce the relative workload on the heart at any given exercise intensity, and improve the body's capacity to transport and use oxygen — reflected in a higher VO₂ max.
