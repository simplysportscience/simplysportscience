Projectile Motion

Why a shot putter doesn't throw at 45 degrees, and what actually determines how far a thrown or kicked object travels.

Key Terms

ProjectileAny object that, once released, moves through the air under the influence of gravity alone.
Angle of ReleaseThe angle at which a projectile is launched; affects the balance of horizontal and vertical distance.
Speed of ReleaseHow fast a projectile is travelling at the moment of release.
Height of ReleaseThe height above the landing point at which a projectile is released.
TrajectoryThe path a projectile follows through the air.

What is a Projectile?

A projectile is any object that, once released, moves through the air under the influence of gravity alone — a thrown javelin, a kicked ball, or a jumping athlete's body.

Factors Affecting Trajectory

  • Angle of release — determines the balance between horizontal and vertical distance travelled.
  • Speed of release — greater release speed increases both horizontal and vertical distance, all else equal.
  • Height of release — a projectile released from a greater height will, all else equal, travel further horizontally before landing.

The Parabolic Flight Path

In the absence of air resistance, a projectile follows a symmetrical, parabolic path. Horizontal velocity stays constant throughout the flight, while vertical velocity is continuously affected by gravity — decreasing on the way up, and increasing on the way down.

Optimal Angle of Release

For a projectile launched and landing at the same height, 45° maximises horizontal distance. But where release height is greater than landing height — such as a shot put or javelin thrown from above the athlete's centre of mass — the optimal angle is slightly less than 45°.

Exam tip: "Analyse the optimal angle of release" questions expect you to mention that release height shifts the ideal angle away from the textbook 45° — a common place marks are lost.

Air Resistance

Air resistance opposes motion through the air, and has a much greater effect on lighter, larger-surface-area projectiles — like a shuttlecock or a discus — than on smaller, denser projectiles like a shot put.

Practice Check
1. Define projectile motion. +
The motion of an object that, once released, travels through the air under the influence of gravity alone.
2. Describe the three factors that affect the trajectory of a projectile. +
Model answer points:
  • Angle of release — affects the balance between horizontal and vertical distance.
  • Speed of release — greater speed increases both horizontal and vertical distance travelled, all else equal.
  • Height of release — a greater release height increases horizontal distance travelled before landing, all else equal.
3. Explain why the optimal angle of release for a shot put is slightly less than 45°. +
Model answer points:
  • The shot is released from above the athlete's centre of mass — i.e. release height is greater than landing height.
  • A slightly lower angle than 45° maximises horizontal distance, by allowing more of the initial velocity to contribute to horizontal motion while still achieving sufficient flight time.
4. Discuss the effect of air resistance on the trajectory of different sporting projectiles. +
Model answer points:
  • Air resistance opposes the motion of a projectile through the air and reduces both its horizontal distance and the symmetry of its flight path.
  • The effect is much greater on projectiles with a large surface area relative to their mass, such as a shuttlecock or discus.
  • Small, dense projectiles like a shot put are affected only slightly by air resistance.
