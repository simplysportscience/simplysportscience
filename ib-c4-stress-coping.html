C.4 Stress and Coping

Why a little nervous energy helps and a lot hurts — and the two genuinely different ways a performer can actually cope with competitive stress.

Key Terms

ArousalA state of physiological and psychological activation, from deep sleep to extreme excitement.
Cognitive AnxietyThe mental component of anxiety — worrying thoughts, negative expectations.
Somatic AnxietyThe physical symptoms of anxiety — racing heart, sweating, muscle tension.
Problem-Focused CopingA coping strategy that addresses the actual source of stress directly.
Emotion-Focused CopingA coping strategy that manages the emotional response to stress, rather than its source.

Arousal and Anxiety

Arousal is a state of activation ranging from deep sleep to extreme excitement — neither good nor bad in itself. Anxiety, a negative emotional state often linked to high arousal, has cognitive (worrying thoughts) and somatic (physical symptoms) components.

The Inverted-U Hypothesis and Catastrophe Theory

The inverted-U hypothesis proposes performance improves with arousal up to an optimal point, then declines — a symmetrical curve. Catastrophe theory challenges this, proposing that once arousal exceeds the optimal point under high cognitive anxiety, performance can drop suddenly rather than gradually.

Sources of Stress

Competitive stress can stem from fear of failure or evaluation, event importance, uncertainty about outcome or ability, and external pressure from coaches, parents or spectators.

Coping Strategies

Two broad approaches to managing stress:

  • Problem-focused coping — directly addressing the source of the stress, e.g. extra preparation or a specific tactical plan to reduce uncertainty.
  • Emotion-focused coping — managing the emotional response itself, e.g. relaxation techniques, reframing thoughts, seeking social support.

Effective coping often combines both — addressing what can genuinely be controlled or prepared for, while managing the emotional response to what can't.

Exam tip: "Discuss coping strategies for a named situation" scores well when you name both problem-focused and emotion-focused approaches and explain which fits the specific stressor described.

Practice Check
1. Describe the difference between cognitive and somatic anxiety. +
Model answer points:
  • Cognitive anxiety refers to worrying thoughts and negative expectations about performance.
  • Somatic anxiety refers to the physical symptoms of anxiety, such as a racing heart, sweating and muscle tension.
2. Explain how catastrophe theory offers an alternative explanation to the inverted-U hypothesis. +
Model answer points:
  • Catastrophe theory proposes that once arousal exceeds the optimal point, particularly under high cognitive anxiety, performance can drop suddenly rather than gradually.
  • It also suggests recovering performance requires arousal to be substantially reduced, rather than simply returning as arousal falls back.
3. Explain the difference between problem-focused and emotion-focused coping. +
Model answer points:
  • Problem-focused coping directly addresses the actual source of stress, e.g. additional preparation to reduce uncertainty.
  • Emotion-focused coping manages the emotional response to stress, e.g. relaxation techniques or reframing negative thoughts.
4. Discuss appropriate coping strategies for an athlete experiencing high anxiety before an important competition. +
Model answer points:
  • Problem-focused strategies, such as thorough tactical preparation, can reduce genuine uncertainty about the event.
  • Emotion-focused strategies, such as controlled breathing or positive self-talk, can manage the physical and cognitive symptoms of anxiety directly.
  • Combining both is often most effective — addressing controllable sources of stress while managing the emotional response to aspects that can't be controlled.
