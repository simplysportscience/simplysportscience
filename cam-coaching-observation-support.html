Coaching & Observation Support

Why a successful shot with bad technique is actually a warning sign, not a win — and what makes coaching feedback genuinely useful.

Key Terms

ObservationSystematically watching a performance to identify strengths and areas for development.
Knowledge of Results (KR)Feedback about the outcome of a performance.
Knowledge of Performance (KP)Feedback about the quality of technique used, regardless of outcome.
Technical ModelAn agreed correct version of a skill, used as a reference point for observation.

Effective Observation

Useful observation isn't passive watching — it involves knowing specifically what to look for, often using a checklist or comparison against an agreed technical model of correct performance, so that observations are consistent and focused rather than a vague general impression.

Types of Feedback

Knowledge of results (KR) tells a performer about the outcome of their action — did the shot go in? Knowledge of performance (KP) tells a performer about the quality of the technique used, regardless of outcome — the shot may have gone in despite a technical flaw that won't work reliably in future. Both are useful, but KP is particularly important for long-term technical development, since outcome alone doesn't always reveal an underlying issue.

Exam tip: "Explain why KP is important even when KR is positive" — a successful outcome doesn't confirm good technique; a flawed technique can still occasionally produce a good result, and KP is what actually catches that.

Effective Coaching Communication

Feedback is most useful when it's specific (identifying exactly what to change, not just "try harder"), appropriately timed (often immediately after a skill for beginners, potentially delayed for more experienced performers who benefit from self-assessment first), and balanced — acknowledging what was done well alongside what needs improvement, to support both technical development and motivation.

Practice Check
1. Define knowledge of performance (KP). +
Feedback about the quality of the technique used during a skill, regardless of the outcome achieved.
2. Describe the difference between knowledge of results and knowledge of performance. +
Model answer points:
  • Knowledge of results tells a performer about the outcome of their action, such as whether a shot was successful.
  • Knowledge of performance tells a performer about the quality of the technique used, regardless of the outcome.
3. Explain why a coach might use a technical model when observing a performer's skill. +
Model answer points:
  • A technical model provides an agreed, correct reference point for the skill being assessed.
  • Comparing the performer's technique against this model allows the coach to identify specific deviations, making observation more consistent and focused than a general impression.
4. Discuss why knowledge of performance feedback is important, even when a skill produces a successful outcome. +
Model answer points:
  • A successful outcome doesn't necessarily confirm that the technique used was correct — a flawed technique can occasionally still produce a good result.
  • Relying only on knowledge of results risks reinforcing a technical fault that happened to work on that occasion, but is unlikely to work reliably in future or under greater pressure.
  • Knowledge of performance feedback allows a coach to identify and correct the underlying technical issue, supporting more reliable long-term improvement.
