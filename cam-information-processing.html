Information Processing

What actually happens in the split second between seeing something on the pitch and reacting to it — and why a well-timed dummy works.

Key Terms

InputSensory information received via the eyes, ears and proprioceptors.
Decision MakingComparing input to memory and selecting an appropriate response.
OutputThe motor programme executed as a result of the decision made.
Reaction TimeThe time between a stimulus appearing and the start of a response.
Psychological Refractory PeriodA delay in processing a second stimulus presented shortly after the first.

The Information Processing Model

A basic model describes how performers process information and respond: input (sensory information is received via the eyes, ears and proprioceptors) → decision making (the information is compared to stored memories and an appropriate response is selected) → output (a motor programme is executed) → feedback (the outcome is evaluated, informing future responses).

Reaction Time, Movement Time & Response Time

Reaction time is the time between a stimulus appearing and the start of the response; movement time is the time taken to actually complete the movement once started; response time is the sum of the two.

The Psychological Refractory Period

When two stimuli are presented in very quick succession, the response to the second is delayed — the psychological refractory period — because the information processing system can only fully process one stimulus at a time (the single channel hypothesis). This underlies the effectiveness of a sporting "dummy" or feint: presenting a first stimulus forces the opponent to begin processing a response, delaying their ability to react to the second, genuine stimulus that follows immediately after.

Exam tip: "Explain why a dummy/feint is effective" is a psychological refractory period question — name the concept directly, don't just describe that it "confuses" the opponent.

Practice Check
1. Define reaction time. +
The time between a stimulus appearing and the start of a performer's response to it.
2. Describe the four stages of the information processing model. +
Model answer points:
  • Input — sensory information is received via the eyes, ears and proprioceptors.
  • Decision making — the information is compared to stored memories and an appropriate response is selected.
  • Output — a motor programme is executed.
  • Feedback — the outcome is evaluated, informing future responses.
3. Explain the single channel hypothesis. +
Model answer points:
  • The information processing system can only fully process one stimulus at a time.
  • If a second stimulus is presented before the first has finished being processed, the response to the second stimulus is delayed.
4. Discuss why a dummy or feint can be an effective tactic in sport, using the concept of the psychological refractory period. +
Model answer points:
  • A dummy presents a first stimulus that the opponent begins to process and respond to.
  • Because the information processing system can only handle one stimulus at a time (the single channel hypothesis), the opponent's ability to process the second, genuine stimulus is delayed.
  • This delay — the psychological refractory period — gives the performer a timing advantage over their opponent.
