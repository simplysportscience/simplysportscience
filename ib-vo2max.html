VO₂ Max & Aerobic Capacity

The number that gets thrown around as the ultimate fitness marker — what it actually measures, how it's tested, and why it isn't the whole story.

Key Terms

VO₂ MaxThe maximum volume of oxygen the body can consume and use per minute, relative to body mass (ml/kg/min).
Direct Gas AnalysisMeasuring VO₂ max via a graded exercise test to exhaustion, analysing inspired/expired gas.
Multi-Stage Fitness TestA sub-maximal, indirect field test that predicts VO₂ max from performance on a progressive shuttle run.
Central AdaptationsImprovements in the heart and blood's capacity to deliver oxygen — e.g. increased stroke volume.
Peripheral AdaptationsImprovements at the muscle itself — e.g. increased capillarisation and mitochondrial density.

What is VO₂ Max?

VO₂ max is the maximum volume of oxygen the body can take in, transport and use per minute, expressed relative to body mass (ml/kg/min). It reflects the combined capacity of the lungs, heart, blood and muscles working together, and is widely used as a marker of aerobic fitness.

Measuring VO₂ Max

Direct measurement uses gas analysis during a graded exercise test to volitional exhaustion — typically on a treadmill or cycle ergometer — measuring the volume of oxygen consumed and carbon dioxide produced as intensity increases.

Indirect (predicted) measurement uses sub-maximal field tests, such as the multi-stage fitness test ("bleep test"), which estimates VO₂ max from performance and heart rate response. These are more practical for testing large groups, but less accurate than direct gas analysis.

VO₂ Max vs. the Lactate Inflection Point

VO₂ max sets an athlete's aerobic ceiling, but the lactate inflection point determines how much of that ceiling can actually be used sustainably. An athlete with a lower VO₂ max but a higher lactate inflection point may out-perform a rival with a higher VO₂ max but a lower threshold, because they can sustain a faster pace for longer before fatigue sets in.

Exam tip: Don't treat VO₂ max as the single determinant of endurance performance — examiners reward answers that also bring in the lactate inflection point and running/cycling economy.

Training to Improve VO₂ Max

Both high-intensity interval training and sustained aerobic training improve VO₂ max, through two categories of adaptation:

  • Central adaptations — increased stroke volume and cardiac output, increased blood volume.
  • Peripheral adaptations — increased capillarisation around trained muscle fibres, and increased mitochondrial density, improving the muscle's own capacity to use the oxygen delivered to it.

Practice Check
1. Define VO₂ max. +
The maximum volume of oxygen the body can consume and use per minute, expressed relative to body mass (ml/kg/min).
2. Describe how VO₂ max is measured directly. +
Model answer points:
  • Via a graded exercise test to volitional exhaustion, typically on a treadmill or cycle ergometer.
  • Gas analysis equipment measures the volume of oxygen consumed and carbon dioxide produced as exercise intensity increases.
3. Explain why a high VO₂ max does not guarantee superior endurance performance. +
Model answer points:
  • VO₂ max represents an athlete's aerobic ceiling or potential, not what can actually be sustained.
  • The lactate inflection point determines what percentage of that ceiling can be sustained for a prolonged period.
  • An athlete with a lower VO₂ max but a higher lactate inflection point may sustain a faster pace for longer than a rival with a higher VO₂ max but a lower threshold.
4. Discuss the factors that account for individual variation in VO₂ max. +
Model answer points:
  • Genetics set an individual's upper ceiling, including factors such as heart size and muscle fibre type distribution.
  • Training status significantly affects VO₂ max, through both central adaptations (e.g. increased stroke volume) and peripheral adaptations (e.g. increased capillarisation).
  • Age — VO₂ max typically peaks in the 20s and gradually declines thereafter.
  • Sex — males typically have higher VO₂ max values than females, partly due to differences in haemoglobin concentration and body composition.
