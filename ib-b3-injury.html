B.3 Injury

What actually causes a sports injury, how to manage one in the crucial first minutes, and the honest question of how much risk sport should carry in the first place.

Key Terms

Acute InjuryAn injury caused by a single, sudden traumatic event.
Chronic InjuryAn overuse injury developing gradually over time.
Intrinsic Risk FactorA risk factor related to the individual, e.g. flexibility or previous injury history.
Extrinsic Risk FactorA risk factor external to the individual, e.g. playing surface or equipment.
POLICE ProtocolProtect, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation — immediate soft tissue injury management.

Causes and Classification of Injury

Injuries can be classified as acute (a single traumatic event, e.g. an ankle sprain) or chronic (gradual overuse, e.g. tendinopathy). Risk factors are intrinsic (individual factors — flexibility, muscle imbalance, previous injury, fitness level) or extrinsic (external factors — playing surface, equipment, weather, level of competition).

Exam tip: "What constitutes an acceptable level of risk in sport?" — a genuine guiding question for this sub-theme — expects you to weigh the inherent risk of physical activity against the real, evidence-based benefits, rather than treating all risk as something to eliminate entirely.

Immediate and Long-Term Management

Immediate management of most acute soft tissue injuries follows the POLICE protocol — Protect, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation — replacing the older, more passive RICE model, since some controlled loading now appears to support better long-term recovery than complete rest. Longer-term management involves a graded, criteria-based return to full training and competition, monitored against symptoms and function rather than a fixed timeline.

Prevention Strategies

Injury prevention targets the specific risk factors relevant to a sport: appropriate warm-up and conditioning to address intrinsic risk factors like flexibility and muscular strength, and appropriate equipment, surfaces and rule enforcement to address extrinsic risk factors.

Practice Check
1. Describe the difference between an acute and a chronic sports injury. +
Model answer points:
  • An acute injury is caused by a single, sudden traumatic event, e.g. an ankle sprain.
  • A chronic injury develops gradually through overuse, e.g. tendinopathy.
2. Explain the difference between intrinsic and extrinsic risk factors for sports injury. +
Model answer points:
  • Intrinsic risk factors relate to the individual, such as flexibility, muscle imbalance, or previous injury history.
  • Extrinsic risk factors are external to the individual, such as playing surface, equipment, or weather conditions.
3. Explain the immediate management of an acute soft tissue injury using the POLICE protocol. +
Model answer points:
  • Protect the injured area from further damage.
  • Apply Optimal Loading — some controlled movement/loading rather than complete rest.
  • Ice to reduce swelling and pain.
  • Compression and Elevation to further limit swelling.
4. Discuss what constitutes an acceptable level of risk in sport. +
Model answer points:
  • All physical activity carries some inherent risk of injury, which must be weighed against its substantial physical and mental health benefits.
  • Acceptable risk depends on context — appropriate safety measures, coaching, and equipment can reduce risk to a reasonable level without removing the activity's core physical demands.
  • A balanced view recognises that eliminating all risk is neither possible nor desirable, since it would also remove much of the challenge and benefit that make sport valuable.
