A.1 Communication

How the body's systems talk to each other, keep conditions stable, and physically move oxygen where it's needed — the three linked ideas behind "communication" in this syllabus.

Key Terms

Nervous SystemThe body's fast, electrical communication pathway, using nerve impulses.
Endocrine SystemThe body's slower, hormonal communication pathway, using chemical messengers in the blood.
HomeostasisMaintenance of a stable internal environment within narrow limits.
Negative FeedbackA self-correcting loop that returns a variable towards its set point.
Cardiac OutputHeart rate × stroke volume; the volume of blood pumped per minute.

A.1.1 Inter-System Communication

The body coordinates its systems using two communication pathways. The nervous system uses fast electrical nerve impulses for rapid, short-duration responses — e.g. the immediate rise in heart rate as exercise begins. The endocrine system uses hormones released into the bloodstream for slower, longer-lasting responses — e.g. adrenaline reinforcing and sustaining the cardiovascular response over the course of a session.

These systems work together rather than separately: a nervous signal can trigger a hormonal release (e.g. sympathetic stimulation of the adrenal medulla releasing adrenaline), and hormones can influence how sensitive the nervous system is to future signals.

Exam tip: "Explain how the nervous and endocrine systems communicate together" wants the interaction between them, not two separate, disconnected descriptions.

A.1.2 Maintaining Homeostasis

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a stable internal environment — temperature, blood glucose, pH — within narrow limits, despite external changes. Most homeostatic control follows a negative feedback loop: a stimulus moves a variable away from its set point → a receptor detects the change → a control centre processes the information → an effector responds, returning the variable toward its set point.

Thermoregulation is the clearest example during exercise: rising core temperature (stimulus) is detected by thermoreceptors, processed by the hypothalamus (control centre), and countered by sweating and vasodilation (effectors), increasing heat loss.

A.1.3 Transport

The cardiovascular and respiratory systems together transport oxygen and nutrients to working tissue and remove waste products.

The heart's four chambers and valves maintain one-way blood flow through pulmonary circulation (to the lungs) and systemic circulation (to the body). Cardiac output (Q) = heart rate × stroke volume, rising from ~5 L/min at rest to 20–40 L/min during maximal exercise. Vascular shunting — vasoconstriction of vessels supplying non-essential organs and vasodilation of vessels supplying working muscle — redirects blood flow where it's needed most.

The respiratory system moves air via pressure changes created by the diaphragm and intercostal muscles, with gas exchange occurring by diffusion across the alveoli. Minute ventilation = tidal volume × breathing rate, rising substantially during exercise to meet increased oxygen demand.

Exam tip: Since this sub-topic explicitly frames cardiovascular and respiratory function as "transport," strong answers connect the two systems directly — e.g. explaining how ventilation changes support the increased oxygen demand created by rising cardiac output — rather than describing each system in total isolation.

Practice Check
1. Describe the negative feedback mechanism involved in thermoregulation during exercise. +
Model answer points:
  • Rising core temperature is the stimulus.
  • Thermoreceptors detect the change.
  • The hypothalamus (control centre) processes the information.
  • Effectors — sweat glands and skin blood vessels — respond via increased sweating and vasodilation, returning temperature toward the set point.
2. Explain how the nervous and endocrine systems work together to communicate the onset of exercise. +
Model answer points:
  • The nervous system triggers an immediate response, e.g. increased heart rate via sympathetic nervous stimulation of the cardiovascular control centre.
  • Sympathetic nervous stimulation of the adrenal medulla triggers the release of adrenaline into the blood.
  • Adrenaline reinforces and sustains the nervous response over a longer duration than nerve impulses alone could.
3. Explain how vascular shunting supports the transport of oxygen during exercise. +
Model answer points:
  • Vasoconstriction narrows blood vessels supplying non-essential organs, such as the gut and kidneys.
  • Vasodilation widens blood vessels supplying working skeletal muscle.
  • This redirects a much greater proportion of cardiac output to the muscles that need oxygen most during exercise.
4. Discuss how the cardiovascular and respiratory systems work together to support the body's transport needs during exercise. +
Model answer points:
  • Minute ventilation rises to bring more oxygen into the alveoli and remove more CO₂, supported by increased tidal volume and breathing rate.
  • Cardiac output rises to transport this oxygen to working muscle more quickly, via increased heart rate and stroke volume.
  • Vascular shunting ensures the increased cardiac output is directed toward the tissues with the greatest oxygen demand.
  • Together, increased ventilation and increased cardiac output form a single, coordinated transport response to rising exercise intensity.
