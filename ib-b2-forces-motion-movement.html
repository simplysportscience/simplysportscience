B.2 Forces, Motion and Movement

The forces acting on every moving body in sport — and how breaking real technique down into its mechanical parts turns into genuinely useful coaching feedback.

Key Terms

InertiaThe tendency of a body to resist a change in its state of motion.
Newton's Second LawForce = mass × acceleration.
DragA fluid resistance force acting opposite to the direction of motion.
Magnus EffectThe curving flight of a spinning ball, caused by pressure differences from spin.
Movement AnalysisSystematically breaking down a technique to identify strengths and areas for development.

B.2.1 Newton's Laws of Motion

First law (inertia) — a body remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted on by an external force. Second law (acceleration) — F = m × a; acceleration is proportional to force and inversely proportional to mass. Third law (reaction) — every action force has an equal and opposite reaction force, e.g. a swimmer's hand pushing water backward propels them forward.

B.2.2 Fluid Mechanics

Drag opposes motion through air or water, increasing with velocity, surface area and fluid density — form drag (from shape/frontal area), surface drag (from friction), and wave drag (at water's surface) can all be reduced through streamlining. Lift acts perpendicular to fluid flow, explained by Bernoulli's principle. The Magnus effect — a spinning ball dragging a boundary layer of air, creating a pressure difference — explains why topspin dips a tennis shot and why a football free kick swerves.

Exam tip: "Explain the Magnus effect" wants the mechanism (spin → boundary layer → pressure difference → curved path), not just "spin makes it curve."

B.2.3 Movement Analysis and Its Applications

Movement analysis applies linear and angular motion concepts — distance, displacement, velocity, acceleration, momentum — to break down a real sporting technique, identifying which forces and mechanical principles are driving (or limiting) performance at each phase of a skill. This is used practically to diagnose technical faults, compare a performer's technique to a model, and inform specific technical coaching points, often using video or biomechanical software to capture the relevant quantities precisely.

Practice Check
1. State Newton's Second Law and explain its application to a sprinter accelerating out of the blocks. +
Model answer points:
  • Force = mass × acceleration.
  • The sprinter's acceleration is proportional to the force applied against the blocks, and inversely proportional to their mass — a greater force relative to mass produces greater acceleration.
2. Explain how the Magnus effect causes a spinning ball to curve. +
Model answer points:
  • Spin drags a thin boundary layer of air around with the ball.
  • On one side, this boundary layer moves with the airflow, reducing pressure; on the other, it moves against the airflow, increasing pressure.
  • The resulting pressure difference creates a net force that curves the ball's flight path toward the low-pressure side.
3. Explain how movement analysis could be used to identify a technical fault in a performer's sprint start. +
Model answer points:
  • The start can be broken into phases and analysed using relevant biomechanical quantities, such as force applied and resulting acceleration.
  • Comparing the performer's data or technique against a model performer highlights specific deviations.
  • This allows a coach to give precise, targeted feedback on a specific phase, rather than a general impression of the whole skill.
4. Discuss how an understanding of fluid mechanics could be used to improve a cyclist's performance. +
Model answer points:
  • Reducing form drag through an aerodynamic riding position lowers the resistance the cyclist must overcome.
  • Reducing surface drag through smooth, tight-fitting clothing further reduces resistance.
  • Since drag increases with velocity, these reductions matter increasingly at higher speeds, where drag becomes the dominant resistive force.
