Theories of Learning

Three competing explanations for how a skill actually gets learned — trial and error, sudden insight, or watching someone else do it — plus the theory of how a stored motor programme adapts to something new.

Key Terms

Motor ProgrammeA generalised series of movements/nerve impulses, stored and initiated as a set, completed in order.
Operant ConditioningLearning through trial and error, shaped by reinforcement and punishment.
Stimulus-Response BondThe learned connection between a situation and a specific response.
Insight LearningLearning through understanding the whole picture, associated with Gestalt theory.
SchemaA generalised rule, built from past experience, used to produce a novel but related movement.

6.1 Motor Programmes

A motor programme is a series of subroutines — a generalised series of movements, represented as a set of nerve impulses sent to the muscles, completed in the correct order. Motor programmes are created, stored and initiated through repeated practice, and can be described using examples broken into their subroutines (e.g. a golf swing: grip → backswing → downswing → follow-through).

6.2 Theories Related to the Learning of Motor Skills

Three theories, each explainable with examples, and each with strengths and limitations to evaluate:

  • Operant conditioning theory — learning through trial and error: shaping behaviour, modifying the environment, and strengthening or weakening the stimulus-response bond through positive reinforcement, negative reinforcement and punishment.
  • Cognitive theory (Gestalt) — learning through understanding the whole picture (Gestaltism), using whole practice and insight learning — a sudden restructuring of understanding rather than gradual trial and error.
  • Bandura's observational learning theory — learning through observing a model, via four stages: attention, retention, motor reproduction, and motivation.

Exam tip: "Evaluate" questions on these theories want a genuine strength AND a genuine limitation for each named theory — not just a description of how it works.

6.3 Schmidt's Schema Theory

Schema theory describes a way of modifying a motor programme to suit new situations, using two types of schema: recall schema (used to select and initiate a movement) and recognition schema (used to evaluate the movement's outcome).

Four rules of schema are used and updated after every movement: knowledge of initial conditions, knowledge of response specifications, sensory consequences, and response outcomes. This is how schema theory helps develop movement skills — allowing a performer to adapt a stored generalised motor programme to a novel but related situation, rather than needing to learn every possible variation from scratch.

Practice Check
1. Explain a motor programme, using an example broken into subroutines. +
Model answer points:
  • A motor programme is a generalised series of movements, represented as a set of nerve impulses to the muscles, completed in the correct order.
  • Example: a tennis serve broken into subroutines — ball toss, racket backswing, contact, follow-through.
2. Explain operant conditioning theory, with reference to the stimulus-response bond. +
Model answer points:
  • Learning occurs through trial and error, shaping behaviour by modifying the environment.
  • Positive reinforcement (a reward following a correct response) and negative reinforcement (removing something unpleasant) strengthen the stimulus-response bond.
  • Punishment following an incorrect response weakens the stimulus-response bond.
3. Evaluate Bandura's observational learning theory as an explanation of skill acquisition. +
Model answer points:
  • Strength: it explains how complex skills can be learned quickly by observing a skilled model, without lengthy trial and error.
  • Strength: identifies specific stages (attention, retention, motor reproduction, motivation) that can be practically applied by coaches choosing how to demonstrate.
  • Limitation: motor reproduction depends on the observer already having the physical capability to perform the skill, which the theory doesn't fully account for.
  • Limitation: motivation to reproduce the behaviour can vary significantly between individuals, limiting the theory's predictive power.
4. Explain how schema theory helps a performer produce a novel but related movement. +
Model answer points:
  • Schema theory proposes that performers store a generalised motor programme, rather than a separate programme for every possible variation of a movement.
  • Recall schema is used to select and initiate an appropriate movement based on past experience.
  • Recognition schema is used to evaluate the outcome, updating the four schema rules (initial conditions, response specifications, sensory consequences, response outcomes) for future use.
  • This allows the performer to adapt the stored schema to a new but related situation, rather than needing to have specifically practised that exact scenario before.
