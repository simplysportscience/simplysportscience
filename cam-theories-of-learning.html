Theories of Learning

Two very different explanations for how skills are actually learned — trial and error versus a sudden flash of understanding.

Key Terms

Operant ConditioningLearning through reinforcement following trial and error.
Law of EffectThorndike's principle that rewarded responses are more likely to be repeated.
Positive ReinforcementProviding a desirable stimulus following a correct response.
Negative ReinforcementRemoving an unpleasant stimulus following a correct response.
Insight LearningLearning through understanding the whole picture, rather than trial and error.

Operant Conditioning (Associationist Theories)

Operant conditioning describes learning through trial and error, shaped by reinforcement. Thorndike's Law of Effect states that responses followed by a satisfying outcome are more likely to be repeated, while those followed by discomfort are less likely to be repeated. Positive reinforcement provides a desirable stimulus (praise, reward) after a correct response; negative reinforcement removes an unpleasant stimulus (e.g. stopping criticism) once a correct response occurs.

Cognitive (Insight) Learning Theory

In contrast, cognitive theories — associated with Gestalt psychology — propose that learning can occur through insight: understanding the whole picture or relationship between elements of a task, sometimes producing a sudden "aha" moment of understanding, rather than gradual trial-and-error improvement.

Exam tip: Examiners like a direct comparison here — operant conditioning suits simple, discrete skills learned through repetition and feedback; insight learning better explains how experienced performers solve novel tactical problems they haven't specifically practised before.

Applying Both Theories in Coaching

A coach might use operant conditioning principles — reinforcement and repetition — to teach a specific technical skill, such as a serve, while relying on insight learning — presenting a tactical problem and letting a player work out the solution — to develop decision-making and game understanding.

Practice Check
1. Define Thorndike's Law of Effect. +
The principle that responses followed by a satisfying outcome are more likely to be repeated, while those followed by an unsatisfying outcome are less likely to be repeated.
2. Describe the difference between positive and negative reinforcement. +
Model answer points:
  • Positive reinforcement involves providing a desirable stimulus, such as praise, following a correct response.
  • Negative reinforcement involves removing an unpleasant stimulus, such as stopping criticism, once a correct response occurs.
3. Explain how insight learning differs from operant conditioning. +
Model answer points:
  • Operant conditioning explains learning as a gradual process of trial and error, shaped by reinforcement.
  • Insight learning proposes that learning can occur through understanding the whole picture or relationship between elements of a task, sometimes producing a sudden moment of understanding rather than gradual improvement.
4. Discuss how a coach might use both operant conditioning and insight learning theory when developing a player. +
Model answer points:
  • Operant conditioning principles — reinforcement and repetition — are well suited to teaching a specific, repeatable technical skill, such as a serving technique.
  • Insight learning is better suited to developing tactical understanding — presenting a player with a game-like problem and allowing them to work out an effective solution, rather than a single prescribed technique.
  • Using both approaches allows a coach to develop technical consistency alongside adaptable decision-making.
