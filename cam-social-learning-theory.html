Social Learning Theory

Why watching someone else perform a skill can teach you almost as much as trying it yourself — if the right person is doing the demonstrating.

Key Terms

Social Learning TheoryLearning through observing and imitating the behaviour of others.
ModellingDemonstrating a behaviour for an observer to imitate.
AttentionThe first stage of Bandura's model — the observer must attend to the model.
RetentionRemembering the observed behaviour for later reproduction.
Motor ReproductionThe physical capability to actually perform the observed behaviour.

Bandura's Social Learning Theory

Social learning theory proposes that much of human behaviour, including sporting skills, is learned through observing and imitating the behaviour of others — a process known as modelling.

The Four Stages of Observational Learning (ARRM)

  1. Attention — the observer must actually attend to the model's behaviour.
  2. Retention — the observed behaviour must be remembered for later reproduction.
  3. Motor reproduction — the observer must have the physical capability to actually perform the behaviour.
  4. Motivation — the observer must be motivated to reproduce the behaviour, often influenced by whether the model's behaviour was reinforced or punished.

What Makes a Model Effective

Observational learning is most effective when the model is significant (someone the observer respects or looks up to), similar to the observer (e.g. similar age, ability level), and of high status — all of which increase the likelihood the observer will actually attend to and be motivated to imitate the demonstrated behaviour.

Exam tip: "Discuss why a coach might choose a particular demonstrator" is testing whether you can name and apply significant, similar, high status — not just say a good demonstration "helps players learn."

Practice Check
1. Define modelling. +
The process of demonstrating a behaviour for an observer to imitate, central to social learning theory.
2. Describe the four stages of Bandura's model of observational learning. +
Model answer points:
  • Attention — the observer must attend to the model's behaviour.
  • Retention — the observed behaviour must be remembered.
  • Motor reproduction — the observer must be physically capable of performing the behaviour.
  • Motivation — the observer must be motivated to reproduce the behaviour.
3. Explain why a model's status can affect how likely an observer is to imitate their behaviour. +
Model answer points:
  • A high-status or significant model is more likely to capture and hold the observer's attention.
  • Observers are also more motivated to imitate a model they respect or admire, increasing the likelihood the behaviour will actually be reproduced.
4. Discuss why a PE teacher might choose a fellow pupil, rather than themselves, to demonstrate a new skill to a class. +
Model answer points:
  • A same-age, similar-ability pupil represents a more similar model to the rest of the class than the teacher, which research suggests increases the effectiveness of observational learning.
  • Observers are more likely to believe they can successfully reproduce a behaviour when the model is similar to themselves, increasing motivation to attempt it.
  • The teacher can still provide instructional feedback around the demonstration, combining the benefits of a similar model with expert guidance.
