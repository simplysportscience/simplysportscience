The Cardiovascular System

How the heart and blood vessels work together at rest — and how the whole system changes the instant you start exercising.

Key Terms

Cardiac Output (Q)Heart rate × stroke volume; the volume of blood pumped by the heart per minute.
Stroke VolumeThe volume of blood ejected from the left ventricle in a single beat.
Vascular ShuntingThe redistribution of blood flow towards working muscle during exercise.
Systolic/Diastolic PressureThe pressure in the arteries during heart contraction / relaxation.
BradycardiaA resting heart rate below 60 bpm, common in endurance-trained athletes.

Heart Structure & the Cardiac Cycle

The heart has four chambers (right/left atrium, right/left ventricle) and four valves that maintain one-way blood flow. The cardiac cycle describes one complete heartbeat: diastole (the heart relaxes and fills with blood) followed by systole (the ventricles contract, ejecting blood into the pulmonary and systemic circulations).

Cardiac Output & the Exercise Response

Cardiac output (Q) = heart rate × stroke volume. At rest, Q is roughly 5 litres/minute; during maximal exercise it can rise to 20-40 litres/minute, driven by rising heart rate and, up to a point, rising stroke volume.

Vascular Shunting

During exercise, vasoconstriction reduces blood flow to non-essential organs (gut, kidneys) while vasodilation increases flow to working muscle, the heart and skin — redirecting the proportion of cardiac output reaching muscle from around 15-20% at rest to over 80% during intense exercise.

Exam tip: Always name vasoconstriction and vasodilation explicitly when answering on vascular shunting — a vague "blood goes to the muscles" answer loses marks.

Blood Pressure Response to Exercise

During dynamic exercise, systolic pressure rises in proportion to intensity while diastolic pressure stays roughly stable or falls slightly. During static/isometric exercise, both systolic and diastolic pressure rise sharply due to sustained muscular compression of blood vessels.

Cardiovascular Adaptations to Training

Endurance training produces cardiac hypertrophy (an enlarged, stronger left ventricle), increased stroke volume at rest and during exercise, bradycardia, and increased capillarisation around trained muscle — together allowing a greater cardiac output at a lower relative effort.

Practice Check
1. Define cardiac output. +
The volume of blood pumped by the heart in one minute, calculated as heart rate × stroke volume.
2. Describe how blood flow is redistributed during exercise. +
Model answer points:
  • Vasoconstriction narrows blood vessels supplying non-essential organs, such as the gut and kidneys.
  • Vasodilation widens blood vessels supplying skeletal muscle, the heart and skin.
  • This redirects a much greater proportion of cardiac output to working muscle.
3. Explain the difference in blood pressure response between dynamic and static exercise. +
Model answer points:
  • In dynamic exercise, systolic pressure rises with intensity while diastolic pressure stays roughly stable or falls slightly.
  • In static exercise, both systolic and diastolic pressure rise sharply, because sustained muscle contraction compresses blood vessels and increases resistance to flow.
4. Discuss the cardiovascular adaptations that result from a period of endurance training. +
Model answer points:
  • Cardiac hypertrophy — the left ventricle enlarges and thickens, increasing pumping capacity.
  • Increased stroke volume at rest and during exercise.
  • Bradycardia — resting heart rate falls, since the same cardiac output can be achieved with fewer beats.
  • Increased capillarisation, improving oxygen delivery and extraction at the muscle.
