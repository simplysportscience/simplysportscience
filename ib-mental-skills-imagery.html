Mental Skills & Imagery

How elite athletes train the mind as deliberately as the body — and the theory behind why simply imagining a movement actually helps you perform it.

Key Terms

ImageryUsing all the senses to create or recreate an experience in the mind, without physically performing it.
Internal ImageryImagining performing a skill from one's own first-person perspective.
External ImageryImagining watching oneself perform, from a third-person perspective.
Psychoneuromuscular TheoryProposes imagery creates small-scale neural/muscular activity similar to the real movement.
Self-TalkThe internal dialogue an athlete has with themselves, which can be motivational, instructional, or negative.

Mental Skills Training

Mental skills training develops the psychological side of performance alongside physical training. Alongside goal setting (covered separately), the core mental skills are imagery, self-talk, relaxation and arousal control, and concentration techniques.

Imagery

Imagery uses all the senses to create or recreate an experience in the mind. Internal imagery involves imagining performing a skill from one's own first-person perspective; external imagery involves imagining watching oneself perform, third-person. Imagery can serve a motivational function (imagining achieving a goal) or a cognitive function (rehearsing a specific skill or strategy).

Why Imagery Works — the Psychoneuromuscular Theory

The psychoneuromuscular theory proposes that vividly imagining a movement creates small-scale neural and muscular activation patterns similar to those produced during actual physical performance — effectively rehearsing the movement pathway even without physical practice. Imagery also builds familiarity and confidence, which can reduce competitive anxiety.

Exam tip: "Explain" questions on imagery want the mechanism named — the psychoneuromuscular theory — not just "it helps you feel more confident."

Self-Talk

Self-talk is the internal dialogue an athlete has with themselves. Motivational self-talk (e.g. "I've trained for this") and instructional self-talk (e.g. "follow through") can both improve performance, while negative self-talk can undermine confidence and increase anxiety.

Relaxation Techniques

Relaxation techniques manage excessive arousal and anxiety — for example, progressive muscular relaxation (systematically tensing then relaxing muscle groups) and controlled, deep breathing. These are particularly useful for bringing arousal back toward an athlete's optimal point, linking directly back to the inverted-U hypothesis.

Practice Check
1. Define imagery. +
A mental technique that uses all the senses to create or recreate an experience in the mind, in the absence of the actual physical performance.
2. Describe the difference between internal and external imagery. +
Model answer points:
  • Internal imagery involves imagining performing the skill from one's own first-person perspective, as if looking through one's own eyes.
  • External imagery involves imagining watching oneself perform from an outside, third-person perspective.
3. Explain how the psychoneuromuscular theory accounts for the benefits of imagery. +
Model answer points:
  • The theory proposes that vividly imagining a movement creates small-scale neural and muscular activation patterns similar to those produced during actual physical performance.
  • This effectively rehearses the movement pathway even without physical practice.
4. Discuss how mental skills such as imagery and self-talk could help an athlete manage pre-competition anxiety. +
Model answer points:
  • Imagery can be used to mentally rehearse a successful performance, building familiarity and confidence, which can reduce cognitive anxiety.
  • Positive or instructional self-talk can replace negative, anxiety-inducing thoughts with constructive focus on process or past success.
  • Combined with relaxation techniques such as controlled breathing, these strategies can help bring arousal back toward an athlete's optimal level, in line with the inverted-U hypothesis.
