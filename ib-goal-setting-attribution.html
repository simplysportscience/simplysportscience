Goal Setting & Attribution Theory

Why the goals you set change how motivated you stay — and why blaming a bad performance on the right thing matters more than you'd think.

Key Terms

SMART GoalsSpecific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time-bound — a framework for effective goal setting.
Outcome GoalA goal focused on the end result, e.g. winning.
Performance GoalA goal focused on improving one's own standard, e.g. a personal best.
Process GoalA goal focused on technique or execution, e.g. maintaining good form.
Attribution TheoryThe explanations people give for the causes of their success or failure.
Locus of CausalityWhether an attribution is internal (about the self) or external (about outside factors).

Goal Setting

Effective goals typically follow the SMART framework — Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time-bound. Goals can also be classified by focus:

  • Outcome goals — focused on the end result, e.g. winning.
  • Performance goals — focused on improving one's own standard, e.g. a personal best time.
  • Process goals — focused on technique or execution, e.g. maintaining good running form.

Performance and process goals tend to be more motivating than outcome goals, since they're within the athlete's own control and don't depend on an opponent's performance.

Why Goal Setting Works

Goal setting provides clear direction and focus, increases effort and persistence, and can reduce anxiety by giving an athlete something concrete and controllable to aim for, rather than a vague sense of "doing well."

Attribution Theory

Attribution theory examines the explanations people give for the causes of their success or failure. Weiner's model classifies attributions along two dimensions — locus of causality (internal vs external) and stability (stable vs unstable) — producing four classic attributions:

InternalExternal
StableAbilityTask Difficulty
UnstableEffortLuck

Attribution and Motivation

Attributing failure to a lack of effort (internal, unstable) is more motivating than attributing it to low ability (internal, stable), because effort can be changed while ability feels fixed. Coaches should encourage athletes to attribute setbacks to controllable, unstable factors — effort, strategy — to protect motivation and confidence going forward.

Exam tip: "Discuss" questions here reward naming Weiner's model directly, and explaining why unstable attributions protect motivation — not just listing the four categories.

Practice Check
1. Define a SMART goal. +
A goal that is Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic and Time-bound.
2. Describe the difference between outcome, performance and process goals. +
Model answer points:
  • An outcome goal focuses on the end result, such as winning.
  • A performance goal focuses on improving one's own standard, such as achieving a personal best time.
  • A process goal focuses on the technique or execution required, such as maintaining a specific running form.
3. Explain why performance and process goals tend to be more motivating than outcome goals. +
Model answer points:
  • Performance and process goals are within the athlete's own control, since they don't depend on the performance of opponents.
  • Outcome goals can be affected by factors outside the athlete's control, such as an opponent's form, which can undermine motivation if the outcome isn't achieved despite a good individual performance.
4. Discuss how a coach could use attribution theory to maintain an athlete's motivation after a defeat. +
Model answer points:
  • Weiner's model classifies attributions by locus of causality (internal/external) and stability (stable/unstable).
  • A coach should encourage the athlete to attribute the defeat to unstable, controllable factors, such as effort or tactics, rather than stable factors like low ability.
  • Unstable attributions suggest the outcome can be changed with a different approach next time, preserving motivation and confidence.
