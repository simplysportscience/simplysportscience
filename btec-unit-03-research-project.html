Research Project in Sport

How to plan, run and honestly evaluate a genuine piece of sport research — from a sharp question to a critical look at your own method.

Learning Aims

ADesign and plan a research project
BConduct research using appropriate methods
CAnalyse and present research findings
DEvaluate research process and outcomes

Key Terms

Primary ResearchNew data collected directly by the researcher, e.g. surveys, interviews, testing.
Secondary ResearchExisting data or literature, gathered by someone else.
SampleThe specific group of people or data selected to represent a wider population.
ValidityWhether a research method actually measures what it claims to measure.
ReliabilityWhether a research method produces consistent results if repeated.

A Designing and Planning a Research Project

A strong research project starts with a clear, specific, answerable question relevant to sport — broad enough to matter, narrow enough to actually investigate within the time available. Planning involves choosing an appropriate methodology, considering practical constraints, and addressing research ethics — informed consent, confidentiality, and safe, appropriate treatment of participants.

B Conducting Research

Primary research — data you collect yourself, such as questionnaires, interviews, observations or fitness testing — is chosen based on what best answers your specific question. Secondary research — existing literature, published data, previous studies — provides context and a benchmark against which to compare your own findings. Sampling decisions (who, and how many, to include) directly affect how much weight your findings can carry.

Assignment tip: Justify your choice of method against your specific research question — "I used a questionnaire because it's quick" won't score well; "I used a questionnaire because it allowed me to gather data from a larger sample than interviews would allow, within my time constraints" will.

C Analysing and Presenting Findings

Quantitative data (numerical) is typically presented using charts, graphs and summary statistics; qualitative data (descriptive, e.g. interview responses) is typically organised by theme. Strong analysis links findings directly back to the original research question, rather than simply describing what the data shows in isolation.

D Evaluating the Research Process

Evaluation means critically reviewing your own research — was your method valid (did it actually measure what you intended) and reliable (would it produce consistent results if repeated)? What were the limitations of your sample size or method, and what would you do differently next time?

Grading Checklist

What Pass, Merit and Distinction actually look like for this unit

1. Design a research project with a clear question and appropriate methodology. Pass+
What this looks like:
  • State a specific, answerable research question, not a broad topic area.
  • Choose and describe a method appropriate to that question.
2. Collect and present research data clearly. Pass+
What this looks like:
  • Use appropriate charts, tables or thematic organisation to present your findings.
  • Ensure your presentation is accurate and easy for a reader to interpret.
3. Analyse your findings, linking them clearly back to your original research question. Merit+
What this looks like:
  • Don't just describe what the data shows — explain what it means in relation to your specific question.
  • Compare your findings to relevant secondary research where possible.
4. Evaluate the validity and reliability of your research process, and suggest specific improvements. Distinction+
What this looks like:
  • Critically assess your own method, not just describe it — identify genuine limitations, such as sample size or potential bias.
  • Suggest concrete, specific improvements, not a vague "I would do more research."
