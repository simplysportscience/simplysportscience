Unit 8 Mandatory ⏱ 120 GLH

Anatomy and Physiology for Exercise and Physical Activity

The body-systems knowledge behind safe, effective exercise instruction — and what actually changes in the body, both during a session and over months of training.

Learning Aims

AUnderstand structure and function of body systems
BApply anatomical knowledge to exercise contexts
CAnalyse physiological responses to exercise

Key Terms

Skeletal SystemProvides support, protection and the framework for movement.
Muscular SystemProduces movement through muscle contraction, working across joints.
Cardiorespiratory SystemThe heart, lungs and blood vessels, responsible for delivering oxygen to working muscle.
Acute ResponseA short-term, immediate change in the body during a single bout of exercise.
Chronic AdaptationA long-term change in the body resulting from repeated training over weeks or months.

A Structure and Function of Body Systems

The skeletal system provides support, protection and the joints needed for movement; the muscular system produces movement by contracting across those joints, working in agonist/antagonist pairs. The cardiorespiratory system — heart, lungs and blood vessels — delivers oxygen to working muscle and removes waste products, underpinning the body's capacity for sustained activity.

B Applying Anatomical Knowledge to Exercise

A fitness instructor needs to translate this knowledge into practice — selecting exercises that correctly target intended muscle groups, understanding safe joint ranges of motion, and recognising when a client's technique risks unsafe stress on a joint or muscle. This applied understanding is what separates simply knowing anatomy from being able to instruct safely and effectively.

Assignment tip: When applying anatomical knowledge, always name the specific muscles, joints and movements involved in an exercise, rather than describing it only in general terms — this specificity is exactly what assessors are looking for.

C Physiological Responses to Exercise

Acute responses occur immediately during a single exercise session — rising heart rate, breathing rate and body temperature. Chronic adaptations build up over weeks and months of consistent training — increased stroke volume, improved muscular strength or endurance, and more efficient thermoregulation. Recognising the difference is essential for setting realistic client expectations and progressing a programme appropriately over time.

Grading Checklist

What Pass, Merit and Distinction actually look like for this unit

1. Describe the structure and function of the skeletal, muscular and cardiorespiratory systems. Pass+
What this looks like:
  • Cover all three systems, with correct anatomical terminology.
  • Describe function, not just structure — what each system actually does, not just what it's made of.
2. Explain how anatomical knowledge should inform safe exercise selection for a client. Merit+
What this looks like:
  • Link specific exercises to the specific muscles and joints they target.
  • Explain how this knowledge reduces injury risk or improves exercise effectiveness.
3. Analyse the acute and chronic physiological responses to a specific type of exercise. Distinction+
What this looks like:
  • Clearly distinguish immediate, single-session responses from adaptations that build up over a training programme.
  • Support your analysis with specific physiological detail, not general statements like "the body gets fitter."
