Linear Motion & Momentum

The vocabulary of movement in a straight line — and why understanding momentum and impulse explains everything from a rugby tackle to a safe landing.

Key Terms

DistanceThe total length of the path travelled, regardless of direction (scalar).
DisplacementThe straight-line distance from start to finish, in a specified direction (vector).
SpeedDistance travelled per unit of time (scalar).
VelocityDisplacement per unit of time, in a specified direction (vector).
MomentumThe product of an object's mass and velocity: momentum = mass × velocity.
ImpulseForce applied over time; equal to the change in momentum. Impulse = force × time.

Distance vs Displacement, Speed vs Velocity

Distance is the total length of the path travelled, regardless of direction — a scalar quantity. Displacement is the straight-line distance from start to finish point, in a specified direction — a vector quantity. The same distinction applies to speed (distance/time, scalar) and velocity (displacement/time, vector).

Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity: a = (v − u) / t. Positive acceleration means speeding up; negative acceleration (deceleration) means slowing down.

Momentum

Momentum = mass × velocity. A heavier or faster-moving object has greater momentum, and is harder to stop or redirect — highly relevant in contact and collision sports like rugby.

Conservation of Momentum & Impulse

In a collision, total momentum before the collision equals total momentum after, within a closed system. Impulse = force × time, and equals the change in momentum. This explains why extending the time over which a force is applied — "following through" when catching a ball, or bending the knees on landing — reduces the peak force experienced for the same overall change in momentum.

Exam tip: "Explain how a performer can reduce injury risk on landing" is really an impulse question — extending the time of force application reduces peak force for the same momentum change.

Practice Check
1. Define momentum. +
The product of an object's mass and its velocity: momentum = mass × velocity.
2. Describe the difference between distance and displacement. +
Model answer points:
  • Distance is the total length of the path travelled, regardless of direction, and is a scalar quantity.
  • Displacement is the straight-line distance between the start and end points in a specified direction, and is a vector quantity.
3. Explain why a rugby player with greater mass and running velocity is harder to stop in a tackle. +
Model answer points:
  • Momentum = mass × velocity, so a player with greater mass and/or higher running velocity has greater momentum.
  • A defender must apply a larger opposing force, or apply force for longer, to bring about the same change in the attacking player's momentum, making them harder to stop.
4. Discuss how understanding impulse can help reduce injury risk when landing from a jump. +
Model answer points:
  • Impulse = force × time, and equals the change in momentum.
  • For a given change in momentum (e.g. from moving downward to stationary), increasing the time over which force is applied reduces the peak force experienced.
  • Bending the knees and allowing a longer landing phase spreads the same impulse over more time, reducing peak impact force on the joints compared to landing stiff-legged.
