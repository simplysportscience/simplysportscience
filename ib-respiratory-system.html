The Respiratory System

How air moves in and out of the body, how oxygen actually gets into the blood, and how breathing changes the moment you start to exercise.

Key Terms

Tidal Volume (TV)The volume of air moved in or out of the lungs in one normal breath.
Vital Capacity (VC)The maximum volume of air that can be forcefully exhaled after a maximum inhalation.
Minute Ventilation (V̇E)The total volume of air breathed in one minute. V̇E = Tidal Volume × Breathing Rate.
AlveoliTiny air sacs at the end of the bronchioles where gas exchange takes place.
DiaphragmThe main muscle of breathing; contracts and flattens during inspiration to increase thoracic volume.
External IntercostalsMuscles between the ribs that contract during inspiration, lifting the rib cage up and out.
ChemoreceptorsSensors that detect changes in blood CO₂ and pH, sending signals to the respiratory centre to adjust breathing.

Structures of the Ventilatory System

Air travels through a sequence of structures on its way to the lungs: the nose and mouth → pharynx → larynx → trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli.

The nose, pharynx and larynx together make up the conducting airways. Their job isn't gas exchange — it's to prepare the air before it reaches the lungs, by:

  • Warming the air to body temperature
  • Moistening (humidifying) the air
  • Filtering out particles and pathogens

Mechanics of Breathing

Breathing happens because of pressure changes inside the thoracic (chest) cavity, created by muscle action:

  • Inspiration (breathing in) — the diaphragm contracts and flattens, and the external intercostal muscles contract, lifting the rib cage up and out. This increases the volume of the thoracic cavity, which decreases the pressure inside it below atmospheric pressure — so air flows in.
  • Expiration (breathing out) — at rest, this is largely passive: the diaphragm and external intercostals relax, the thoracic cavity gets smaller, pressure rises above atmospheric, and air flows out. During exercise, expiration becomes active, using the internal intercostal muscles and abdominal muscles to forcefully push air out faster.

Exam tip: A common error is describing inspiration and expiration as symmetrical processes. At rest they aren't — inspiration is always active, but resting expiration is passive. Only exercise expiration becomes active.

Gas Exchange at the Alveoli

Gas exchange happens by diffusion — gases move from an area of high concentration to low concentration, across the thin walls of the alveoli and the surrounding capillaries.

  • Oxygen concentration is higher in the alveoli than in the blood, so oxygen diffuses into the blood.
  • Carbon dioxide concentration is higher in the blood than in the alveoli, so CO₂ diffuses out of the blood into the alveoli, ready to be exhaled.

This exchange is efficient because the alveoli have a huge total surface area, extremely thin walls (one cell thick), and a dense network of surrounding capillaries.

Lung Volumes

  • Tidal Volume (TV) — air moved in one normal, relaxed breath (~500ml at rest).
  • Inspiratory Reserve Volume (IRV) — the extra air that can be forcefully inhaled after a normal breath in.
  • Expiratory Reserve Volume (ERV) — the extra air that can be forcefully exhaled after a normal breath out.
  • Residual Volume (RV) — the air that always remains in the lungs, even after maximum exhalation.
  • Vital Capacity (VC) — the maximum volume that can be exhaled after a maximum inhalation (TV + IRV + ERV).
  • Total Lung Capacity (TLC) — vital capacity plus residual volume.

Control of Breathing & the Exercise Response

Breathing rate and depth are controlled by the respiratory centre in the medulla oblongata. At rest, this is driven mainly by chemoreceptors detecting rising CO₂ levels and falling pH in the blood.

During exercise, ventilation increases in two phases:

  • An immediate, anticipatory rise at the very start of exercise, driven by neural signals from the motor cortex and receptors in the working muscles — before any chemical change has even occurred.
  • A continued rise as exercise progresses, now driven by chemoreceptors responding to the actual increase in CO₂ production and drop in pH from working muscles.

Both tidal volume and breathing rate increase, which increases minute ventilation — sometimes from around 6 L/min at rest to over 150 L/min during maximal exercise in trained athletes.

Practice Check
1. Define minute ventilation. +
The total volume of air breathed in one minute, calculated as tidal volume multiplied by breathing rate (V̇E = TV × breathing rate).
2. Describe the mechanics of inspiration at rest. +
Model answer points:
  • The diaphragm contracts and flattens.
  • The external intercostal muscles contract, lifting the rib cage up and out.
  • These actions increase the volume of the thoracic cavity.
  • This decreases pressure inside the thorax below atmospheric pressure, so air flows in.
3. Explain how gas exchange occurs at the alveoli. +
Model answer points:
  • Gases move by diffusion, from high to low concentration.
  • Oxygen concentration is higher in the alveoli than the blood, so oxygen diffuses into the blood.
  • CO₂ concentration is higher in the blood than the alveoli, so CO₂ diffuses out into the alveoli to be exhaled.
  • This is made efficient by the alveoli's large surface area, thin walls and rich capillary supply.
4. Discuss how ventilation changes during exercise. +
Model answer points:
  • Ventilation rises in two phases: an immediate anticipatory increase driven by neural signals, before any chemical change has occurred.
  • A continued, more gradual rise driven by chemoreceptors detecting increased CO₂ and decreased pH from working muscles.
  • Both tidal volume and breathing rate increase, raising minute ventilation substantially.
  • Expiration also becomes active, using internal intercostal and abdominal muscles to expel air faster.
