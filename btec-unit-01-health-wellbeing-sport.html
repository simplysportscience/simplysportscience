Health, Wellbeing and Sport

Why sport and physical activity matter for both body and mind — and how to properly screen, interpret and feed back on someone's health status.

Learning Aims

AExamine the importance of physical activity and sport
BInvestigate the importance of physical health
CExplore mental health and social wellbeing
DUndertake health and wellbeing screening and provide feedback

Key Terms

Physical RecreationActivity done in leisure time for enjoyment, e.g. walking or cycling.
Physical HealthThe ability of the body to function effectively.
Mental HealthEmotional wellbeing and resilience to adversity.
Normative DataPopulation benchmarks used to judge whether a test result is typical or a cause for concern.
Health ScreeningA structured process of testing and questioning used to assess someone's current health status.

A The Importance of Physical Activity and Sport

Active pursuits fall into several categories: sport (competitive activity using skills and tactics), physical recreation (leisure activity like walking or cycling), outdoor activities (e.g. climbing, kayaking), physical education (curriculum-based activity), and physical fitness (activity aimed at improving fitness, e.g. resistance training, yoga).

Different participant groups — young people, older adults, people with a disability or medical condition, those at risk of social isolation — benefit differently from these activities, both physically (heart health, healthy body composition, muscular strength) and mentally (self-esteem, reduced loneliness, improved social wellbeing).

Organisations provide sport and physical activity for social, financial, environmental and historical reasons — from improving community health and reducing crime, to generating revenue and preserving green space for recreation.

B The Importance of Physical Health

Physical health is the ability of the body to function effectively — moving freely, managing daily tasks, and being free of unexplained pain. It's affected by activity levels, diet, sleep, stress, smoking and alcohol use, among other factors.

Physical health is typically benchmarked using health monitoring tests with recognised normative data: blood pressure, resting heart rate, and body composition measures such as BMI or bioelectrical impedance.

Assignment tip: When assessing your own or a client's physical health, always compare the result against recognised normative data, not just your own opinion of whether it "seems okay" — that comparison is exactly what separates a Pass-level description from a Merit-level analysis.

C Mental Health and Social Wellbeing

Mental health is emotional wellbeing and resilience to adversity; social wellbeing is the ability to function in society and form relationships. Both can be affected by factors such as bereavement, long-term stress, social isolation, or a long-term physical health condition.

Common signs of poor mental health and social wellbeing include reduced motivation, difficulty in personal relationships, increased irritability, and changes to sleep or appetite. Wellbeing can be measured using recognised scales, structured conversation with health professionals, or observed lifestyle indicators such as emotional resilience and social interactivity.

D Health and Wellbeing Screening

Effective screening starts with building rapport and communicating clearly and sensitively with a client, since health questions can be personal. Testing should follow correct protocols and maintain the client's dignity throughout. Results should always be interpreted against normative data or accepted health ranges, before identifying practical strategies to improve health status — and feeding this back constructively, highlighting strengths as well as areas for improvement.

Grading Checklist

What Pass, Merit and Distinction actually look like for this unit

1. Explain the different types of physical activity provided in a local area and their benefits. Pass+
What this looks like:
  • Identify at least one example from each category — sport, physical recreation, outdoor activity, PE, physical fitness.
  • Link each to a specific physical or mental health benefit, not just a general statement that "exercise is good for you."
2. Analyse how different activities can benefit different groups of participants in a local area. Merit+
What this looks like:
  • Go beyond describing benefits in general — connect specific activities to specific participant groups.
  • Explain why that particular activity suits that particular group (e.g. low-impact activity for older adults, social team sport for those at risk of isolation).
3. Evaluate the reasons for providing different activities in a local area, recommending ways to engage more participants. Distinction+
What this looks like:
  • Weigh up the social, financial, environmental and historical reasons for provision, rather than just listing them.
  • Base recommendations on a genuine gap or weakness you've identified in current local provision — vague, generic suggestions won't reach Distinction standard.
4. Use national physical health benchmarks to assess your own physical health. Pass+
What this looks like:
  • Carry out the specified health monitoring tests correctly.
  • Record your results and state the relevant normative data range for comparison.
5. Analyse your own physical and mental health against national normative data, identifying strengths and areas for improvement. Merit+
What this looks like:
  • Don't just state the numbers — explain what each result means for your health.
  • Identify specific, evidenced strengths and specific, evidenced areas for improvement, not a generic "I should exercise more."
6. Evaluate your own current physical and mental health, including the potential impact if no improvements are made. Distinction+
What this looks like:
  • Discuss realistic consequences of not addressing identified areas for improvement — this is what distinguishes evaluation from analysis.
  • Where relevant, link consequences specifically to performance, daily life or long-term health, not just a vague "it would be bad."
