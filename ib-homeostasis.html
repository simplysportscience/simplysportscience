Homeostasis

How the body keeps its internal environment stable — and what happens to that balance the moment you start exercising.

Key Terms

HomeostasisThe maintenance of a stable internal environment within narrow limits, despite changes in external conditions.
Negative FeedbackA self-regulating mechanism where a change in a variable triggers a response that brings it back towards its set point.
ReceptorA structure that detects a change in a monitored variable, such as core temperature or blood glucose.
EffectorA muscle or gland that carries out the response that restores a variable towards its set point.
VasodilationWidening of blood vessels, increasing blood flow — e.g. to the skin, to increase heat loss.
VasoconstrictionNarrowing of blood vessels, reducing blood flow — e.g. to the skin, to conserve heat.

What is Homeostasis?

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a stable internal environment — including body temperature, blood glucose, pH and blood pressure — within narrow limits, despite changes in the external environment or the demands placed on the body.

The Negative Feedback Mechanism

Most homeostatic control follows the same basic model:

  1. Stimulus — a variable moves away from its normal set point.
  2. Receptor — detects the change.
  3. Control centre — processes the information (often the hypothalamus).
  4. Effector — a muscle or gland brings the variable back towards its set point.

Thermoregulation During Exercise

Exercise generates heat as a by-product of muscle contraction, causing core temperature to rise. The body responds with vasodilation of skin blood vessels, increasing heat loss through radiation and convection, and increased sweating, which cools the body as sweat evaporates. In hot or humid conditions, sweat can't evaporate as efficiently, increasing the risk of heat illness.

Exam tip: Examiners want the full loop named — stimulus → receptor → control centre → effector → response — not just a general description of "the body cooling down."

Homeostatic Challenges During Exercise

Temperature regulation isn't the only homeostatic challenge during exercise. Rising blood lactate and falling pH, progressive glycogen depletion, and fluid loss through sweat all push key variables away from their resting set points — and the body's improving capacity to manage these challenges is a major part of what training adaptation actually is.

Practice Check
1. Define homeostasis. +
The maintenance of a stable internal environment within narrow limits, despite changes in external conditions.
2. Describe the negative feedback mechanism involved in thermoregulation during exercise. +
Model answer points:
  • Rising core temperature is the stimulus.
  • Thermoreceptors detect the change.
  • The hypothalamus (control centre) processes the information.
  • Effectors — sweat glands and skin blood vessels — respond via increased sweating and vasodilation, increasing heat loss and returning temperature toward the set point.
3. Explain why exercise challenges homeostatic balance. +
Model answer points:
  • Muscle contraction generates heat, raising core temperature.
  • Energy metabolism produces lactate and H⁺ ions, lowering blood pH.
  • Sweating causes fluid and electrolyte loss.
  • Glycogen stores are progressively depleted — all pushing key variables away from their resting set points.
4. Discuss how the ability to maintain homeostasis during exercise changes with training status. +
Model answer points:
  • Trained individuals begin sweating at a lower core temperature and sweat more efficiently, improving heat dissipation.
  • Trained individuals buffer lactate more effectively.
  • Improved glycogen storage capacity allows homeostatic balance to be maintained at higher exercise intensities than in untrained individuals.
