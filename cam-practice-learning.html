Practice and Learning

Not all practice is equal — how the right type of practice, guidance and motivation changes depending on the performer and the stage of learning they're at.

Key Terms

Massed PracticeContinuous practice with minimal rest between attempts.
Distributed PracticePractice broken up with rest intervals between attempts.
Stages of LearningCognitive, associative and autonomous — the progression of skill acquisition.
Transfer of LearningThe effect of learning one skill on the learning of another.
Intrinsic MotivationMotivation from within — enjoyment, satisfaction, mastery.

8.1 Types of Practice

Six types, each with advantages and disadvantages to evaluate:

  • Whole — practising a skill in its entirety.
  • Part — breaking a skill into components.
  • Whole–part–whole — practising the whole skill, isolating a weak component, then returning to the whole.
  • Progressive part — practising components separately, then linking them together progressively.
  • Massed — continuous practice with minimal rest; distributed — practice with rest intervals.
  • Fixed — repeating a skill in a stable, unchanging environment; varied — practising a skill across changing conditions.

Exam tip: "Evaluate" questions on practice types want both an advantage AND a disadvantage, tied to a specific type of skill or performer — not a one-sided answer.

8.2 Types of Guidance

Four types, each with advantages and disadvantages:

  • Visual — demonstration, video.
  • Verbal — spoken explanation.
  • Manual — physically guiding a performer through a movement.
  • Mechanical — using equipment (e.g. a harness) to support or guide movement.

8.3 Stages of Learning

Three stages, each with distinct characteristics:

  • Cognitive — early stage; understanding what to do, frequent large errors, high reliance on guidance and feedback.
  • Associative — refining technique; fewer, more consistent errors.
  • Autonomous — movement is largely automatic, requiring little conscious thought.

You need to evaluate how feedback, practice and guidance types should each be used differently at different stages — e.g. visual/verbal guidance and massed, fixed practice suit the cognitive stage; mechanical guidance and varied, distributed practice suit later stages.

8.4 Transfer of Learning

Transfer of learning is the effect of learning one skill on the learning of another. Six types:

  • Positive — one skill aids the learning of another.
  • Negative — one skill hinders the learning of another.
  • Zero — no effect between skills.
  • Proactive — a previously learned skill affects a new one.
  • Retroactive — learning a new skill affects a previously learned one.
  • Bilateral — transfer from one limb to the other.

Exam tip: Strategies to optimise positive transfer and limit negative transfer are explicitly examinable — be ready to suggest specific coaching strategies, not just define the transfer types.

8.5 Motivation

Intrinsic motivation comes from within — enjoyment, satisfaction, mastery. Extrinsic motivation comes from external rewards, which can be tangible (medals, money) or intangible (praise, applause). You need to evaluate both types — intrinsic motivation tends to be more sustainable long-term, but extrinsic rewards can support motivation, particularly for beginners or in the short term.

Practice Check
1. Describe the difference between massed and distributed practice. +
Model answer points:
  • Massed practice involves continuous practice with minimal rest between attempts.
  • Distributed practice involves practice broken up with rest intervals between attempts.
2. Describe the characteristics of the cognitive stage of learning. +
Model answer points:
  • The performer is focused on understanding what to do.
  • Errors are frequent and often large.
  • The performer relies heavily on guidance and feedback from a coach.
3. Explain the difference between positive and negative transfer of learning, using examples. +
Model answer points:
  • Positive transfer occurs when learning one skill aids the learning of another, e.g. a tennis serve aiding a badminton serve.
  • Negative transfer occurs when learning one skill hinders the learning of another, e.g. a tennis groundstroke technique interfering with a squash shot.
4. Evaluate the use of intrinsic and extrinsic motivation to develop a beginner performer. +
Model answer points:
  • Intrinsic motivation, built through enjoyment and early success, tends to produce more sustainable long-term commitment to a sport.
  • Extrinsic rewards, such as praise or small tangible rewards, can help maintain motivation in the early cognitive stage, when intrinsic enjoyment may not yet be established.
  • Over-reliance on extrinsic rewards risks undermining intrinsic motivation over time, so should be used to support, not replace, intrinsic drivers.
