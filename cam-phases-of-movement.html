Phases of Movement

Why a flawed follow-through is often a symptom, not the actual problem — breaking a skill down into its three working parts.

Key Terms

Preparation PhaseThe set-up before a skill is executed.
Execution PhaseThe main action producing the skill's outcome.
Recovery PhaseThe follow-through and return to a ready position after execution.

The Three Phases of a Skill

Most sporting skills can be broken into three phases:

  • Preparation phase — the set-up before the main action, e.g. a bowler's run-up, or a golfer's backswing.
  • Execution phase — the main action that produces the skill's outcome, e.g. the moment of ball release or club-to-ball contact.
  • Recovery phase — the follow-through and return to a balanced, ready position, e.g. a golfer's follow-through, or a server's recovery ready for the next shot.

Why the Phases Matter

Errors in an earlier phase often cause problems later — a poor preparation phase (e.g. an unbalanced backswing) frequently causes a flawed execution phase, even if the athlete's technique in the execution phase itself looks correct in isolation. Coaches use phase analysis to isolate exactly where in a skill a technical fault originates.

Exam tip: "Analyse" questions using video/phase breakdown want you to identify which specific phase a fault occurs in, and explain the knock-on effect on the following phase(s) — not just describe what looks wrong overall.

Applying Phase Analysis: a Golf Swing

Preparation — stance, grip and backswing set the base for the whole shot. Execution — the downswing and point of contact determine ball flight and distance. Recovery — the follow-through, often overlooked, reflects whether the preceding phases were balanced and controlled; a rushed or incomplete follow-through is frequently a symptom of a fault earlier in the swing, not a separate problem in itself.

Practice Check
1. Define the execution phase of a skill. +
The main action within a skill that produces its outcome — for example, the moment of contact between bat and ball, or the release of a thrown object.
2. Describe the three phases of movement in a tennis serve. +
Model answer points:
  • Preparation — the ball toss and racket back-swing, setting up the shot.
  • Execution — the moment of racket-to-ball contact.
  • Recovery — the follow-through and return to a balanced, ready position for the next shot.
3. Explain why a fault in the preparation phase of a skill often affects the execution phase. +
Model answer points:
  • The preparation phase establishes the body position, balance and timing needed for a correct execution.
  • An unbalanced or poorly-timed preparation phase reduces the athlete's ability to execute the skill correctly, even if their technique during execution would otherwise be sound.
4. Discuss how a coach could use phase analysis to identify and correct a technical fault in a performer's skill. +
Model answer points:
  • Breaking the skill down into preparation, execution and recovery phases allows a coach to isolate exactly where in the movement a fault originates, rather than only observing the overall outcome.
  • A fault visible in a later phase, such as a rushed follow-through, may actually stem from an earlier phase, such as an unbalanced preparation.
  • Video analysis is often used to slow down and review each phase individually, allowing more precise, targeted coaching feedback.
