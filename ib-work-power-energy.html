Work, Power & Energy

Why holding a static handstand technically does zero "work" — and how a pole vaulter turns a sprint into height.

Key Terms

WorkForce applied × distance moved in the direction of the force (measured in Joules).
PowerThe rate of doing work: power = work ÷ time (measured in Watts).
Kinetic EnergyThe energy of motion: ½ × mass × velocity².
Potential EnergyStored energy due to position: mass × gravity × height.
Conservation of EnergyEnergy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred between forms.

Work

Work = force × distance moved in the direction of the force, measured in Joules. Importantly, no mechanical work is done if there's no displacement — an isometric muscle contraction holding a static position does zero mechanical work, even though it requires real muscular effort and energy expenditure.

Exam tip: This is a common trap: a question describing a static hold (e.g. a gymnast holding a handstand) is testing whether you know that zero displacement means zero mechanical work — even though the athlete is clearly working hard.

Power

Power = work ÷ time, measured in Watts. A more powerful athlete does the same amount of work in less time, or more work in the same time — the key quality behind explosive actions like sprinting, jumping and Olympic lifting.

Kinetic & Potential Energy

Kinetic energy (energy of motion) = ½ × mass × velocity². Potential energy (stored energy due to position) = mass × gravity × height. A high jumper or pole vaulter converts kinetic energy built up from running speed into gravitational potential energy as they rise.

The Work-Energy Principle & Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred between forms. A pole vaulter demonstrates this clearly: kinetic energy from the run-up is converted into elastic potential energy as the pole bends, then into gravitational potential energy as the vaulter rises over the bar — the same total energy, moving through different forms.

Practice Check
1. Define power. +
The rate of doing work, calculated as power = work ÷ time, measured in Watts.
2. Describe how work is calculated, and explain why an isometric muscle contraction does zero mechanical work. +
Model answer points:
  • Work = force × distance moved in the direction of the force.
  • Because an isometric contraction involves no movement or displacement, no mechanical work is done, even though muscular effort and energy expenditure are involved.
3. Explain the difference between kinetic and potential energy. +
Model answer points:
  • Kinetic energy is the energy of motion, calculated as ½ × mass × velocity².
  • Potential energy is stored energy due to position, such as gravitational potential energy, calculated as mass × gravity × height.
4. Discuss how a pole vaulter demonstrates the conservation of energy during a vault. +
Model answer points:
  • Kinetic energy built up during the run-up is converted into elastic potential energy as the pole bends on planting.
  • As the pole straightens, this elastic potential energy converts into gravitational potential energy as the vaulter rises.
  • Throughout the vault, energy is transferred between forms rather than created or destroyed, in line with the principle of conservation of energy.
