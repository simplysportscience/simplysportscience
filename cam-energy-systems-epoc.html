Energy Systems & EPOC

How the body regenerates ATP during exercise — and why you keep breathing hard for minutes after you've already stopped.

Key Terms

ATP-PC SystemAnaerobic system using stored phosphocreatine; fast but lasts ~10 seconds.
Anaerobic GlycolysisBreakdown of glucose without oxygen, producing lactic acid.
Aerobic SystemUses oxygen to break down carbohydrate/fat via the Krebs cycle; slow but high-yield.
Oxygen DeficitThe gap at the start of exercise where oxygen demand exceeds supply.
EPOCExcess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption; elevated oxygen intake continuing after exercise.

The Three Energy Systems

SystemOxygen?DurationATP Yield
ATP-PCAnaerobic~10 secondsVery low, very fast
Anaerobic GlycolysisAnaerobic~10-60 secondsLow (2 ATP), fast
AerobicAerobic60 seconds+High (36-38 ATP), slow

The ATP-PC system regenerates ATP almost instantly from stored phosphocreatine. Anaerobic glycolysis breaks down glucose without oxygen, producing lactic acid as a by-product. The aerobic system fully breaks down carbohydrate and fat via the Krebs cycle and electron transport chain, yielding far more ATP but far more slowly.

Oxygen Deficit

At the start of exercise, oxygen demand outpaces supply while the cardiovascular and respiratory systems "catch up" — this gap is the oxygen deficit, and it explains why anaerobic systems dominate the opening seconds of any exercise bout.

EPOC — Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption

After exercise, oxygen consumption stays elevated above resting levels for a period — EPOC. This has two components:

  • Fast component — replenishing phosphocreatine stores and myoglobin/haemoglobin oxygen stores; largely complete within a few minutes.
  • Slow component — clearing lactate, and supporting elevated body temperature, heart rate and hormone levels; can take considerably longer.

Exam tip: Make sure you can name and explain both components of EPOC separately — many students only mention lactate clearance and miss the fast component entirely.

Practice Check
1. Define EPOC. +
Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption — the elevated oxygen intake that continues after exercise, above resting levels, to restore the body to its pre-exercise state.
2. Describe the two components of EPOC. +
Model answer points:
  • The fast component replenishes phosphocreatine stores and myoglobin/haemoglobin oxygen stores, and is largely complete within a few minutes.
  • The slow component involves clearing lactate and supporting elevated body temperature, heart rate and hormone levels, and can take considerably longer.
3. Explain why oxygen deficit occurs at the start of exercise. +
Model answer points:
  • Oxygen demand rises immediately at the onset of exercise.
  • The cardiovascular and respiratory systems take time to increase oxygen delivery to match this demand.
  • This shortfall forces greater reliance on anaerobic energy systems in the opening seconds of exercise.
4. Discuss the relative contribution of the three energy systems during a 400m race. +
Model answer points:
  • The ATP-PC system dominates the opening seconds, providing rapid but short-lived ATP.
  • Anaerobic glycolysis becomes the dominant system for the remainder of the race, given its duration of roughly 45-60 seconds.
  • The aerobic system makes only a small contribution, since there isn't sufficient time for it to become the primary supplier of ATP.
