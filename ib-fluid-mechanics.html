Fluid Mechanics — Drag & Lift

Why cyclists tuck, swimmers wear skin suits, and free kicks bend — the forces air and water exert on every moving body.

Key Terms

DragA fluid resistance force that acts opposite to the direction of motion through air or water.
Form DragDrag arising from the shape and frontal surface area of a moving object.
Surface (Skin) DragDrag arising from friction between a fluid and the surface of a moving object.
LiftA force acting perpendicular to fluid flow, created by a pressure difference.
Magnus EffectThe curving flight path of a spinning ball, caused by pressure differences from the spin dragging the boundary layer of air.

Drag Force

Drag acts opposite to the direction of motion through a fluid (air or water), and increases with velocity, surface area, and fluid density. There are several types relevant to sport:

  • Form drag — from the shape and frontal area of an object; reduced by adopting a more streamlined body position, such as a cyclist's aerodynamic tuck.
  • Surface (skin) drag — from friction between the fluid and the object's surface; reduced with smooth, tight-fitting clothing such as skin suits.
  • Wave drag — relevant in swimming, created at the water's surface; reduced by staying streamlined and swimming beneath the surface during a push-off.

Lift Force

Lift acts perpendicular to the direction of fluid flow, and is created by a pressure difference across an object — explained by Bernoulli's principle, where faster-moving fluid over a surface creates lower pressure than slower-moving fluid beneath it, generating a net force.

The Magnus Effect

When a ball spins, it drags a thin boundary layer of air around with it. On one side, this boundary layer moves with the surrounding airflow, reducing air pressure; on the other side, it moves against the airflow, increasing air pressure. The resulting pressure difference creates a force that curves the ball's flight path towards the low-pressure side — the Magnus effect. This explains topspin dipping a tennis shot, backspin extending a lob's flight, and the swerve on a football free kick.

Exam tip: "Explain" questions on the Magnus effect want the mechanism (boundary layer → pressure difference → net force), not just "spin makes it curve."

Reducing Drag in Practice

Athletes and equipment designers reduce drag by streamlining body position, using specialised low-drag clothing and equipment, and — in cycling and skiing — adopting aerodynamic postures that reduce frontal surface area without compromising the power an athlete can produce.

Practice Check
1. Define drag. +
A fluid resistance force that acts opposite to the direction of motion through a fluid such as air or water.
2. Describe the difference between form drag and surface drag. +
Model answer points:
  • Form drag arises from the shape and frontal surface area of an object moving through a fluid, and is reduced by adopting a more streamlined body position.
  • Surface (skin) drag arises from friction between the fluid and the surface of the object, and is reduced with smooth clothing or equipment, such as a skin suit.
3. Explain how the Magnus effect causes a spinning ball to curve. +
Model answer points:
  • Spin drags a thin boundary layer of air around with the ball.
  • On one side, this boundary layer moves with the surrounding airflow, reducing air pressure.
  • On the other side, it moves against the airflow, increasing air pressure.
  • The resulting pressure difference creates a net force that curves the ball's flight path towards the low-pressure side.
4. Discuss how athletes reduce drag to improve performance. +
Model answer points:
  • Adopting a streamlined, low-frontal-area body position, such as a cyclist's aerodynamic tuck, reduces form drag.
  • Wearing smooth, tight-fitting clothing such as skin suits reduces surface drag.
  • In swimming, a streamlined push-off and body position beneath the surface reduces wave drag, which is greatest at the water's surface.
