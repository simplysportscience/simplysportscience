Energy Systems

How the body regenerates ATP for muscle contraction — and why a 100m sprinter, a rugby player and a marathon runner rely on completely different energy systems to fuel their performance.

Key Terms

ATPAdenosine triphosphate — the only usable form of energy for muscle contraction.
ATP-PC SystemAnaerobic system that regenerates ATP using stored phosphocreatine (PC); fast but very short-lasting.
Anaerobic GlycolysisThe breakdown of glucose/glycogen to pyruvate without oxygen, producing lactic acid as a by-product.
Aerobic SystemUses oxygen to break down carbohydrates and fats via the Krebs cycle, producing far more ATP but more slowly.
Oxygen DeficitThe gap at the start of exercise where oxygen demand exceeds oxygen supply, forcing reliance on anaerobic systems.
EPOCExcess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption — the elevated oxygen intake that continues after exercise to restore the body to resting state.

ATP: The Only Fuel Muscles Can Use

Whatever food you eat — carbohydrate, fat or protein — it has to be converted into ATP (adenosine triphosphate) before it can be used, because ATP is the only molecule muscle cells can use directly for contraction. Energy is released when the bond holding the final phosphate group is broken: ATP → ADP + P + energy.

The problem is that muscles only store a tiny amount of ATP — enough for about 1–2 seconds of maximal work. Everything after that depends on how quickly the body can resynthesise ATP, and that's where the three energy systems come in.

System 1: The ATP-PC System

Phosphocreatine (PC) is a high-energy compound stored in the muscle. When ADP levels rise, the enzyme creatine kinase breaks down PC, releasing energy that's used to rebuild ATP almost instantly.

PC → phosphate + creatine + energy → energy used to convert ADP back into ATP.

This system is anaerobic (no oxygen needed) and extremely fast, but stores are limited — enough to fuel maximal effort for roughly 10 seconds. It's the dominant system for a shot put throw, a tennis serve, or the first few strides of a 100m sprint.

System 2: Anaerobic Glycolysis (the Lactic Acid System)

Once PC stores run low, the body turns to glycolysis — breaking down glucose or stored glycogen. Without oxygen present, the end product (pyruvate) is converted into lactic acid rather than being fully broken down.

This system produces ATP quickly, but the yield is small (around 2 ATP per glucose molecule), and the build-up of lactic acid contributes to the muscular fatigue felt during high-intensity efforts. It dominates for efforts lasting roughly 10–60 seconds — a 400m sprint being the classic example.

System 3: The Aerobic System

For longer, lower-intensity work, the body uses oxygen to fully break down carbohydrates and fats:

  • Glucose is broken down to pyruvate via glycolysis.
  • In the presence of oxygen, pyruvate enters the Krebs cycle in the mitochondria.
  • The electron transport chain then produces a large yield of ATP — around 36–38 ATP per glucose molecule (far more than anaerobic glycolysis).
  • Fats can also be broken down aerobically, yielding even more ATP, but at a slower rate.

This system is slower to respond, but can sustain ATP production for hours — the system a marathon runner relies on almost entirely.

Exam tip: "Compare" or "discuss" questions on the three systems are really asking you to weigh up speed vs. yield — the ATP-PC system is fastest but smallest, the aerobic system is slowest but by far the largest. Structure your answer around that trade-off.

Oxygen Deficit & EPOC

At the start of any exercise, oxygen demand outstrips supply — the cardiovascular and respiratory systems take time to "catch up." This gap is the oxygen deficit, and it's why the body relies on anaerobic systems in the opening seconds of exercise, even for activities that will end up being mostly aerobic.

After exercise ends, oxygen consumption doesn't immediately return to resting levels — this is EPOC (Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption). The extra oxygen is used to replenish PC stores, clear lactic acid, and restore the body to a resting state.

Relative Contribution of Each System

In practice, all three systems are active at once — it's the balance between them that shifts depending on exercise intensity and duration. A 400m sprint relies mostly on the ATP-PC and anaerobic glycolytic systems, with the aerobic system contributing a small but growing share. A marathon relies almost entirely on the aerobic system, with ATP-PC and glycolysis playing only a minor role, mainly at the very start or during a late sprint finish.

Practice Check
1. Define cell respiration. +
The process by which cells break down fuel sources (carbohydrates, fats, and to a lesser extent protein) to produce ATP, the usable form of energy for muscle contraction.
2. Describe the resynthesis of ATP by the ATP-PC system. +
Model answer points:
  • Rising ADP levels are detected by the enzyme creatine kinase.
  • Creatine kinase breaks down stored phosphocreatine (PC) into phosphate and creatine, releasing energy.
  • This energy is used to rebuild ATP from ADP and phosphate.
  • The system is anaerobic and very fast, but PC stores are limited to around 10 seconds of maximal effort.
3. Explain the phenomena of oxygen deficit and EPOC. +
Model answer points:
  • Oxygen deficit occurs at the start of exercise, when oxygen demand exceeds the supply the cardiovascular and respiratory systems can currently provide.
  • This forces greater reliance on anaerobic energy systems in the opening seconds of exercise.
  • EPOC (Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption) is the continued elevated oxygen intake after exercise ends.
  • This extra oxygen is used to replenish phosphocreatine stores, clear accumulated lactic acid, and return the body to its resting state.
4. Discuss the relative contributions of the three energy systems during a 400m sprint compared to a marathon. +
Model answer points:
  • A 400m sprint (roughly 45–60 seconds) draws heavily on the ATP-PC system in the opening seconds, then shifts to anaerobic glycolysis as the dominant system for the remainder of the race.
  • The aerobic system makes only a small contribution during a 400m race, since there isn't time for it to become the dominant supplier.
  • A marathon relies almost entirely on the aerobic system throughout, since it can sustain ATP production for hours using fats and carbohydrates.
  • ATP-PC and anaerobic glycolysis play only a minor role in a marathon — mainly at the very start, or during a finishing sprint.
