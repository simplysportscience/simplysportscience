Skill and Ability

Why a skill isn't just 'hard' or 'easy' — the six real scales examiners expect you to place any skill on, and the innate abilities that sit underneath it.

Key Terms

Skill ContinuumA scale used to classify a skill between two extremes, e.g. fine to gross.
Fine SkillA skill requiring precise control of small muscle groups.
Gross SkillA skill involving large muscle groups, requiring less precision.
Innate AbilityAn ability that is genetically inherited, present from birth.
Underpinning AbilityAn ability that provides the foundation for developing several different skills.

5.1 Classification of Skills

Skills are classified using six continua — you must be able to justify where a skill sits on each:

ContinuumExtremes
Muscular involvementFine ↔ Gross
Environmental influenceOpen ↔ Closed
ContinuityDiscrete ↔ Serial ↔ Continuous
PacingExternally paced ↔ Internally paced
DifficultyBasic ↔ Complex
OrganisationHigh organisation ↔ Low organisation

Exam tip: "Justify the classification of a named skill" wants you to place it on multiple continua with reasoning for each — a single-word answer like "it's open" won't score well without justification.

5.2 Abilities

Abilities are distinct from skills — they are the underlying traits that support skill development. Three characteristics:

  • Innate — genetically inherited, present from birth (e.g. reaction time capacity).
  • Underpinning — provide the foundation for developing multiple different skills.
  • Enduring — stable over time, not easily changed through practice.

Exam tip: Don't confuse ability with skill — skills are learned and can be improved through practice; abilities are the largely fixed, innate traits that make learning certain skills easier or harder.

Practice Check
1. Explain the muscular involvement continuum, using examples. +
Model answer points:
  • A fine skill requires precise control of small muscle groups, e.g. a dart throw.
  • A gross skill involves large muscle groups and less precision, e.g. a squat jump.
2. Justify the classification of a tennis serve using the pacing and environmental influence continua. +
Model answer points:
  • A tennis serve is self-paced (internally paced), since the server controls when to begin the action.
  • It is relatively closed, since the environment (ball position, court) is largely predictable and controlled by the performer, though opponent positioning adds some open elements.
3. Explain the three characteristics of ability. +
Model answer points:
  • Innate — genetically inherited, present from birth.
  • Underpinning — provides the foundation for developing several different skills.
  • Enduring — stable over time and not easily changed through practice.
4. Discuss why understanding a performer's abilities is important for talent identification. +
Model answer points:
  • Because abilities are innate and enduring, they set a ceiling on which skills a performer can realistically develop to a high level.
  • Identifying underpinning abilities early (e.g. reaction time, coordination) allows coaches to predict potential across a range of related skills, not just one.
  • Since abilities are difficult to change through practice, talent identification programmes often prioritise testing for abilities over currently-demonstrated skill level.
